Thailand remains one of the world's best-value travel destinations. It is also not as cheap as it was ten years ago, and some corners of it — luxury resort islands, internationally-branded restaurants, tourist-zone tuk-tuks — are priced for people who stopped checking the exchange rate.

The gap between what a careful traveller spends and what an incautious one spends is larger in Thailand than almost anywhere else I know. Here are actual figures for 2026, by travel style.

All prices in USD. Exchange rate approximately 36 THB to 1 USD at time of writing.

## Budget Travel: $40–70 Per Person Per Day

This is not backpacker deprivation. It is a comfortable, genuine experience of Thailand with local transport, local food, and well-run guesthouses.

**Accommodation:** $15–25/night. In Bangkok, this gets you a clean, air-conditioned room with private bathroom in a well-located guesthouse in areas like Silom or Ari. On Koh Samui's quieter beaches, similar money gets you a bungalow with a fan and shared facilities — acceptable for one night, uncomfortable for a week.

**Food:** $10–15/day. Eating from markets and local restaurants where Thais eat. Pad Thai from a street stall is 60–80 baht (under $2.50). A full meal at a local restaurant is 100–150 baht. Add a coffee and breakfast: $5/day total is realistic. Going upmarket to tourist-facing restaurants in the same area adds $5–8 per meal.

**Transport:** $5–8/day. BTS and MRT in Bangkok are cheap and efficient. Songthaews (shared pick-up trucks) on the islands are 30–50 baht. Grab taxis for longer journeys.

**Activities:** $5–15/day. Temple entry is free or minimal. National parks are $5–8 entry. A half-day cooking class runs $25–35. A day trip to Ayutthaya from Bangkok, including transport, is $20–30.

**A 10-day trip at budget travel style:** $500–750 per person excluding flights.

## Mid-Range Travel: $100–180 Per Person Per Day

This is where most international leisure travellers sit — comfortable hotels, good restaurants, private transport for longer journeys, a mix of organised tours and independent time.

**Accommodation:** $50–100/night. A three-star hotel in a good Bangkok location (Sukhumvit, Silom) with pool access, a proper breakfast, and reliable air conditioning. On the islands, this reaches boutique resorts with a sea view and a reasonable beach. Samitivej-area Sukhumvit has several hotels in this range that are ideal for medical tourism stays.

**Food:** $30–50/day. A mix of local restaurants and mid-range international dining. Bangkok has exceptional value at this level — you can eat extremely well at $15–25 per meal, including wine, at restaurants that would cost three times as much in London or Sydney.

**Transport:** $15–20/day. Private taxi for most journeys in Bangkok, occasional BTS, a private transfer to the airport.

**Activities:** $20–40/day. Guided half-day tours, day trips with private driver, entry to attractions.

**A 10-day trip at mid-range:** $1,200–2,000 per person excluding flights.

## Comfortable/Luxury Travel: $250–600+ Per Person Per Day

Bangkok and Thailand's resort islands have some of the world's best luxury hotels at prices that still undercut equivalent quality in Europe or the US.

**Accommodation:** $150–400/night. Bangkok has properties like The Capella, Rosewood, and the Mandarin Oriental that are genuinely among the finest hotels in the world — and priced at 40–60% of what the equivalent would cost in London, Paris, or New York. On Koh Samui, a private pool villa at a Ritz-Carlton or Six Senses property runs $300–600/night.

**Food:** $80–150/day. Bangkok's fine dining scene is extraordinary for the price. A tasting menu at a top Bangkok restaurant costs $60–100 per person — a fraction of equivalent quality in Western capitals.

**Transport:** $30–60/day. Private car on call, occasional boat charter.

**Activities:** $50–150/day. Private tours, chartered day boats to islands, cooking classes with top chefs, premium spa treatments.

**A 10-day luxury trip:** $3,000–6,500 per person excluding flights.

## What Most Travellers Get Wrong

**Booking tourist-facing transport.** Metered taxis and Grab are cheaper than anything your hotel will arrange, with one exception: airport transfers. Arranging a private airport pickup is worth it for arrivals — the metered taxi queue at Suvarnabhumi can be 30 minutes at peak hours.

**Eating in hotel restaurants.** Hotel restaurants in Bangkok, including at good hotels, are consistently overpriced for what they deliver. Walk five minutes in any direction in Sukhumvit and the food improves and the price drops.

**Over-scheduling.** The days you remember in Thailand are not the ones with three activities booked. Bangkok moves at its own pace and rewards time spent in unexpected places. Budget empty time.

**Not accounting for the Songkran effect.** If you are visiting in April, check whether your dates overlap with Songkran (Thai New Year water festival, usually 13–15 April, extended celebrations across the week). Accommodation prices double, streets in key areas are deliberately inundated with water, and transport is limited. It is an extraordinary experience if you want it, chaotic if you do not.
