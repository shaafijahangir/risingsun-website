The best Thai food in Bangkok is not in a restaurant.

This is not a provocative claim — it is a straightforward geographical fact about how Bangkok's food culture works. The dishes that have made Thai cuisine one of the world's great food traditions are street dishes and market dishes, developed over generations by vendors who have done nothing else their entire working lives.

After twenty years eating across this city, here is where the food actually is.

## Or Tor Kor Market: The Standard

Or Tor Kor is a covered fresh market adjacent to Chatuchak, in the northern part of the city. It is one of Bangkok's best-kept open secrets — exceptionally well organised (for a market), clean, and stocked with produce and prepared food at a quality level that makes the tourist-facing restaurants near the Grand Palace look like airport food.

**What to eat here:**

**Mango sticky rice** — khao niao mamuang. The mangoes at Or Tor Kor are selected to a standard you will not find elsewhere. The coconut cream is thick and just salted. If you eat this anywhere in Bangkok first, everywhere else will disappoint you. Go in the morning when the mangoes are freshest.

**Som tam** (green papaya salad) — eaten in three regional styles at Or Tor Kor: classic Central Thai (fish sauce, lime, tomatoes, peanuts), Isaan style (adding fermented fish paste and land crabs), and a milder tourist version if you want it. Point at the style you want; the vendor will ask your spice tolerance.

**Grilled pork skewers** (mu ping) — the smell will find you. Pork belly on bamboo skewers, grilled over charcoal, eaten with sticky rice. One of Bangkok's canonical street foods.

**Getting there:** BTS to Mo Chit station (Northern end of the Sukhumvit line), then a five-minute walk or short taxi. Open daily.

## The Street Food Breakfast Bangkok Actually Eats

Tourists eat at restaurants. Locals eat standing up, at street carts, before 9am.

**Jok** (rice congee) is Bangkok's classic breakfast — a thick, creamy rice porridge topped with minced pork, a soft egg, ginger, and crispy fried garlic. It is deeply comforting, filling, and costs approximately 50 baht. Cart vendors selling jok set up before dawn and are often sold out by 9am.

**Patongo** (Chinese-style fried dough) — eaten hot from the oil, dipped into pandan-flavoured coconut custard (sangkaya) or plain condensed milk. Not a health food. Extraordinary with coffee.

**Khanom krok** — small coconut milk pancakes, crispy on the outside and creamy-soft in the centre. Eaten directly off the griddle. One of the most distinctive Thai street foods, almost entirely absent from restaurant menus.

These foods require finding the right cart rather than the right restaurant. The Ari neighbourhood (BTS Ari) has excellent street food carts that serve the local commuter population in the morning. The area around Chatuchak Park is excellent on weekday mornings.

## The 7-Eleven Question

Every guide to Bangkok eventually has to address this: is the food at 7-Eleven actually good?

The honest answer is yes, some of it is, and pretending otherwise is food snobbery.

The **toasted sandwiches** (particularly ham and cheese, or tuna) are made fresh throughout the day and are genuinely good for 30 baht. The **steamed buns** (salapao) are better than most Western equivalents. The **Thai milk tea** from the self-serve machine is exactly as sweet and comforting as it should be.

The 7-Eleven in Thailand is not a convenience store in the Western sense — it is a part of daily food culture. The quality of prepared items is meaningfully higher than 7-Eleven equivalents in the US or UK. Using it for breakfast or a quick lunch is not a concession; it is participating.

That said: do not eat a 7-Eleven lunch instead of the market food above. This is a supplement, not a substitute.

## Chatuchak Weekend Market: Beyond the Shopping

Most visitors to Chatuchak go for the shopping. The food inside the market is equal to the shopping, and less crowded because fewer people know about it.

**Jay Fai** — Bangkok's most famous street cook has her original restaurant near Wat Saket, but the dishes she made famous are available across the city's markets. **Crab omelette** (kai jeow poo) — a lacy, deep-fried omelette folded around fresh crab meat — is the pinnacle of Thai street food technique.

**Phat Thai** at Chatuchak is made by vendors who have been doing this for thirty years. Order the version with fresh prawns (kung sot), not frozen. Watch it being made — the wok technique is part of the experience.

**Tom saap** — a northern-Isaan sour soup, more herbaceous and acidic than tom yum, with pork ribs. It should feel like being punched very pleasantly in the face by galangal and lime. Available at the northeastern Thai food section of the market.

## The Dishes That Never Make Tourist Menus

**Larb moo** — a minced pork salad with toasted rice powder, fresh herbs, fish sauce, and lime. Often made with raw pork at authentic Isaan restaurants (larb dip). The cooked version is extraordinary. The raw version is not for the uninitiated.

**Khao kha moo** — braised pork leg over rice, served with pickled vegetables and a soft-boiled egg. A one-dish meal. It should be eaten at a specialist vendor — someone who has been braising pork in the same clay pot for years. Costs approximately 60 baht.

**Khao tom** — rice soup, the lighter version of jok, eaten late at night by Thais as a restorative after a long evening. Not on restaurant menus because it is not profitable. Deeply comforting at midnight.

**Boat noodles** (kuay teow reua) — small bowls of intense, dark noodle broth traditionally sold from boats on Bangkok's canals. Now available at specialist vendors, particularly around the Victory Monument area. The broth is made with pork blood and is richer and more complex than any tourist-facing noodle dish.

## How to Find Good Food in Bangkok

The rules are simple:

Follow the line. If there is a queue of locals at a cart at 7am, get in it. The queue is the review.

Look for the single-dish vendor. Someone who has been making khao kha moo for thirty years makes better khao kha moo than a restaurant that makes fifteen dishes.

Eat where the prices are in Thai. Menus with English prices are often double what the Thai-language equivalent charges for the same dish.

Avoid the restaurant photograph menus near major temples. The food exists to feed tourists who do not know better. It is not where you eat.

The best Bangkok food experiences I have had — after twenty years — have been at carts with no English signs, ordered by pointing, eaten standing up at 8am. Nothing in any restaurant at any price point has reliably matched them.
