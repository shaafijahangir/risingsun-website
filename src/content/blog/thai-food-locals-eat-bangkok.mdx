The best Thai food in Bangkok is not in a restaurant. The dishes that made Thai cuisine one of the world's great food traditions are street dishes and market dishes, made by vendors who have done nothing else their entire working lives. Here is where to find them.

## Or Tor Kor Market

Or Tor Kor is a covered fresh market adjacent to Chatuchak in the north of the city. It is exceptionally well organised, clean, and stocked with produce and prepared food at a quality level that makes tourist-facing restaurants near the Grand Palace look like airport food.

**Mango sticky rice** (khao niao mamuang): The mangoes here are selected to a standard you will not find elsewhere. The coconut cream is thick and lightly salted. If you eat this here first, everywhere else will disappoint you. Go in the morning when the mangoes are freshest.

**Som tam** (green papaya salad): Available in three styles at Or Tor Kor. Classic Central Thai uses fish sauce, lime, tomatoes, and peanuts. Isaan style adds fermented fish paste and land crabs. A milder tourist version if you need it. Point at the style you want. The vendor will ask your spice level.

**Mu ping** (grilled pork skewers): The smell will find you. Pork belly on bamboo skewers grilled over charcoal, eaten with sticky rice. One of Bangkok's canonical street foods.

**Getting there:** BTS to Mo Chit station, five-minute walk or short taxi. Open daily.

## What Bangkok Eats for Breakfast

Tourists eat at restaurants. Locals eat standing up at street carts before 9am.

**Jok** (rice congee): Bangkok's classic breakfast. A thick rice porridge topped with minced pork, a soft egg, ginger, and crispy fried garlic. Deeply comforting, filling, and about 50 baht. Cart vendors set up before dawn and often sell out by 9am.

**Patongo** (fried dough): Eaten hot from the oil, dipped into pandan-flavoured coconut custard or condensed milk. Not a health food. Extraordinary with coffee.

**Khanom krok**: Small coconut milk pancakes, crispy outside and creamy-soft in the centre. Eaten directly off the griddle. One of the most distinctive Thai street foods, almost entirely absent from restaurant menus.

These require finding the right cart, not the right restaurant. The Ari neighbourhood (BTS Ari) has excellent street food carts serving the local commuter population in the morning. The area around Chatuchak Park is good on weekday mornings.

## Chatuchak Weekend Market: The Food Section

Most visitors come for the shopping. The food inside the market is equal to the shopping, and less crowded because fewer people know about it.

**Crab omelette** (kai jeow poo): A lacy, deep-fried omelette folded around fresh crab meat. The pinnacle of Thai street food technique.

**Phat Thai**: Made by vendors who have been doing this for thirty years. Order the version with fresh prawns (kung sot), not frozen. Watch the wok technique. It is part of the experience.

**Tom saap**: A northern-Isaan sour soup, more herbaceous and acidic than tom yum, made with pork ribs. It should hit you very pleasantly in the face with galangal and lime. Available at the northeastern Thai food section of the market.

## Dishes That Never Appear on Tourist Menus

**Larb moo**: Minced pork salad with toasted rice powder, fresh herbs, fish sauce, and lime. The cooked version is extraordinary. The raw version (larb dip) is not for the uninitiated.

**Khao kha moo**: Braised pork leg over rice, served with pickled vegetables and a soft-boiled egg. A complete one-dish meal. It should come from a specialist vendor who has been braising pork in the same pot for years. About 60 baht.

**Khao tom**: Rice soup, the lighter version of jok, eaten late at night by Thais as a restorative after a long evening. Not on restaurant menus because it is not profitable. Deeply comforting at midnight.

**Boat noodles** (kuay teow reua): Small bowls of intense, dark broth traditionally sold from boats on Bangkok's canals. Now available at specialist vendors, particularly around the Victory Monument area. The broth is made with pork blood and is richer and more complex than any tourist-facing noodle dish.

## How to Find Good Food

Follow the line. A queue of locals at a cart at 7am is the only review you need.

Look for the single-dish vendor. Someone who has spent thirty years making khao kha moo makes better khao kha moo than any restaurant serving fifteen dishes.

Eat where prices are written in Thai. Menus with English prices are often double what the Thai-language equivalent charges for the same dish.

Avoid the photo-menu restaurants near major temples. They exist to feed tourists who do not know better.

The best Bangkok meals I have had after twenty years have been at carts with no English signs, ordered by pointing, eaten standing up at 8am.
