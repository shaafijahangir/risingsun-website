Package tours solve a real problem: too many decisions, not enough confidence, and limited time to research a place you have never been. They are a standardised product, assembled around what an operator can contract at volume, not around what is best for you.

Both options have their place.

## What a Package Tour Gives You

A Thailand package from a major operator typically includes return flights, hotel at a pre-contracted property, airport transfers, some guided tours, and a customer service contact.

The pricing looks straightforward. The hotel was chosen because the operator has a commercial relationship with it. The restaurants on guided dinners were chosen the same way. The tour guide is whoever is available.

None of this makes a package bad. It makes it predictable.

## What a Custom Itinerary Gives You

- Hotels chosen because they are right for your dates, budget, and location priorities
- A schedule that reflects your pace, whether that means serious temples and street food or two beach days and a cooking class
- Private transport where it makes sense, public transport where it does not
- Restaurants recommended because they are excellent, not because they pay for referrals
- A contact who has been to every place on your itinerary

The tradeoff is a conversation. You need to articulate what you want.

## The Price Comparison

A 10-day Bangkok plus Chiang Mai trip for two people in April:

**Package tour (major UK operator, per couple):**
- Flights: included (economy, indirect, two stops)
- Hotel: 4-star in central Bangkok and central Chiang Mai
- Transfers and two guided day tours: included
- Total: £3,800–4,500 per couple

**Custom itinerary (same quality):**
- Flights: direct via Bangkok Air or Thai Airways, £900–1,100 per person: £1,800–2,200 per couple
- Hotels: comparable 4-star properties, booked directly: £80–120/night for 9 nights: £720–1,080
- Private airport transfers: £40–60 each way
- Two private day tours with driver and guide: £90–140 per tour
- Total: £2,700–3,600 per couple

The custom version is typically £400–900 cheaper per couple at the same quality level. The gap widens on longer trips.

Packages can win on last-minute bookings when operators discount unsold inventory, on complex multi-destination logistics, and for very budget-focused trips where volume contracts produce lower hotel rates than independent booking.

## What Packages Cannot Do Well

**They cannot flex the pace.** The schedule is the schedule. If you want three more days in Chiang Mai, you cannot have them.

**They cannot account for what you specifically value.** A food-obsessed traveller and a temple-obsessed traveller get the same programme.

**They cannot access non-obvious options.** The restaurants, guesthouses, and local experiences that make Thailand extraordinary for someone who knows it well are not in any brochure.

**They cannot flex around medical requirements.** For anyone combining a holiday with a hospital appointment or procedure in Bangkok, no package operator handles this. The logistics require someone who understands both sides.

## When a Package Makes Sense

Your first trip to a destination where independent logistics feel overwhelming. A package removes the decision paralysis.

A trip where seeing the key sights and coming home is the goal, not a particular kind of experience.

Group travel where herding ten people through independent bookings is not how you want to spend your holiday.

## When Custom Makes Sense

Any trip where you know what you want and package constraints would force compromises you do not want.

Medical tourism. A package operator cannot coordinate your hospital appointments, recovery stay, and leisure time around a procedure.

Repeat visitors who have done the package version and want something more.

Anyone whose preferences are specific, whether that is food, accommodation type, or pace.
