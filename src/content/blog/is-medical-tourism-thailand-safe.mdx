Bangkok hospitals are safe. Here is what that actually means.

## What JCI Accreditation Means

Joint Commission International is the same body that accredits hospitals in the United States. JCI accreditation requires meeting over a thousand standards: patient safety, clinical care, staff qualifications, infection control, medication management.

Bumrungrad, Samitivej, and BangPakok 9 are all JCI-accredited. Bumrungrad has held accreditation since 2002 and has been re-accredited multiple times.

Practically: these hospitals are audited against international standards, not just Thai ones. Surgeons are more likely to have trained abroad than at a rural Thai hospital. Nursing ratios, equipment protocols, and infection control are benchmarked against global practice. No accreditation guarantees every outcome. But you are not gambling on an unknown institution.

## Language and Communication

At Bumrungrad, the international patient team covers 30+ languages. Dedicated coordinators handle Arabic, Japanese, Korean, Bangla, and major South Asian languages. Most senior physicians trained in the UK, US, or Australia and speak English fluently. Discharge notes are issued in English as standard.

At Samitivej and BangPakok 9, English coverage is strong at consultant level and good at nursing level. Occasional gaps can appear on night shifts or with junior staff. This is why having a local contact reachable at any hour matters. I am contactable throughout every client's stay.

## If a Complication Happens

In hospital during the post-operative period, the infrastructure is there. Bumrungrad operates a 24-hour, 60-bed ICU. Samitivej's cardiac ICU has international fellowship-trained cardiologists. Both hospitals have on-call anaesthesiologists, blood bank services, and 24-hour emergency intervention capacity.

The harder scenario is a complication that appears after you fly home. Your GP did not perform the surgery and may not have your Bangkok records. This is manageable with the right preparation:

**Get comprehensive discharge documentation.** JCI hospitals issue discharge summaries, operative notes, implant records, and post-operative protocols in English. Send these to your home GP immediately on return. I arrange this as standard.

**Understand your follow-up protocol before leaving.** Your Bangkok surgeon should give you a clear timeline: when to get a wound check, what blood work to repeat, and which symptoms require an emergency call versus a GP visit.

**Use telemedicine.** Bumrungrad and Samitivej both offer post-operative video consultations. A call with your Bangkok surgeon is available within 24–48 hours and costs less than a private appointment at home.

**Have the right insurance.** Not holiday insurance. Medical travel insurance that specifically covers complications from a planned procedure abroad. I can point you to providers that cover this.

## The Clinics to Avoid

Some Bangkok clinics look like hospitals. They are not. They are not JCI-accredited, surgeon credentials are unverifiable, and safety records are not audited. These are the operations behind most medical tourism horror stories.

Bumrungrad is a 580-bed tertiary care hospital with 30+ specialist centres. A shopfront clinic on a tourist street is not the same category. Every hospital I work with is JCI-accredited. That is not negotiable.

## The Actual Risk Profile

You are having a medical procedure in a foreign country. The hospital is accredited. The surgeons are qualified. Complication rates for common procedures at Thai hospitals are comparable to Western benchmarks. A knee replacement at Bumrungrad carries similar risk to one at a London teaching hospital.

The genuine risks are logistical. Being far from home when something worrying happens. A GP who does not have your Bangkok records. Flying too soon. Wrong insurance. Choosing a hospital on price alone.

All of those risks are manageable. They are also exactly what a local coordinator handles.
