After helping thousands of international patients choose a Bangkok hospital over two decades, the most common first question I get is some version of: *how do I know nothing will go wrong?*

It is the right question. It deserves an honest answer — not a reassuring brochure.

Here is what I actually know, after twenty years of watching this work well and occasionally watching it not.

## The Short Answer

Yes. Medical tourism in Thailand is safe — provided you use the right hospitals, have realistic expectations about what safe means, and understand that safety in Bangkok looks different from safety at home.

The long answer matters more.

## What JCI Accreditation Actually Means

When you see a hospital advertising JCI accreditation, that is not marketing language. Joint Commission International is the same organisation that accredits hospitals in the United States. Getting accredited requires meeting over a thousand standards covering patient safety, clinical care, staff qualifications, infection control, and medication management.

Bumrungrad International, Samitivej Sukhumvit, and BangPakok 9 — the three hospitals I recommend most often — are all JCI-accredited. Bumrungrad has maintained JCI accreditation since 2002 and has been re-accredited multiple times. Samitivej achieved it in 2009.

What this means in practical terms: these hospitals are audited against international standards, not just Thai ones. A surgeon at Bumrungrad is more likely to have trained abroad (UK, US, Australia) than one at a rural Thai hospital. The nursing ratios, the equipment protocols, and the infection control standards are benchmarked against global best practice.

It does not mean every outcome is guaranteed. No accreditation does. But it means you are not gambling on an unknown institution.

## The Language Situation

This is the fear people rarely say out loud: *what if I cannot communicate when something is wrong?*

At Bumrungrad, the international patient services team covers 30+ languages. There are dedicated coordinators for Arabic, Japanese, Korean, Bangla, and all major South Asian languages. Most senior physicians trained in the UK, US, or Australia and speak functional to fluent English. Discharge notes and post-operative instructions are issued in English as standard.

At Samitivej and BangPakok 9, the English coverage is strong at consultant level and good at nursing level. Gaps can occasionally exist at night shift or with junior staff — which is why having a local contact who can be called at 2am is not a luxury but a practical safeguard.

At every hospital I work with, I am contactable throughout my clients' stays. That is not a sales point — it is the reason the service exists.

## What Happens If There Is a Complication

This is the question underneath every other question, and it deserves a direct answer.

Thai hospitals are equipped to handle surgical complications at the same level as a major Western hospital. Bumrungrad operates a 24-hour critical care unit with 60+ ICU beds. Samitivej's cardiac ICU is one of the most advanced in Southeast Asia. If something goes wrong in theatre or in the immediate post-operative period, the infrastructure to manage it is there.

The more complex scenario is a complication that emerges *after* you have returned home.

Here is what that typically looks like: you have flown back to the UK or US, you develop an infection or an unexpected pain, and you are now sitting in front of a GP who did not perform the surgery and has limited information about it. This is not a uniquely Bangkok problem — the same would happen if you had surgery in another city in your home country. But it is a real consideration.

What good preparation looks like:

**Get comprehensive discharge documentation.** Thai hospitals at JCI-accredited level provide detailed discharge summaries, operative notes, histology reports, and post-operative instructions. This documentation should go to your home GP immediately on return. I arrange this as standard for every client.

**Understand your follow-up protocol before you leave.** Your Bangkok surgeon should give you a clear timeline: when to get a wound check, what blood work to repeat, what to watch for. Ask specifically what is in the "call us immediately" category and what is in the "see your GP" category.

**Use telemedicine.** Bumrungrad and Samitivej both offer post-operative telemedicine consultations. If something concerns you at home, a video consultation with your Bangkok surgeon costs a fraction of a private appointment in the UK and is available within 24–48 hours.

**Have proper travel insurance.** Not holiday insurance — medical travel insurance that covers complications arising from a planned procedure abroad. This is a specific product. I can point you toward providers who cover this.

## The Scam Question

A small number of clinics in Bangkok are not hospitals. They look like clinics, they operate as clinics, and they attract medical tourists with prices that seem competitive. They are not JCI-accredited, their surgeons' credentials are difficult to verify, and their safety records are not publicly audited.

These operations are why medical tourism in Thailand gets a bad reputation it does not deserve at the hospital level.

The distinction matters: Bumrungrad International is a 580-bed tertiary care hospital with more than 30 specialist centres. A clinic on a tourist street is not. If someone is offering you cosmetic surgery in a building you walked past on your way to a night market, that is not the same category of care.

Every hospital I work with is JCI-accredited. That is not a negotiable part of how I operate.

## The Risk You Are Actually Taking

The honest version of the safety picture is this:

You are having a medical procedure in a foreign country. The hospital is accredited. The surgeons are qualified. The outcomes data for Thai hospitals is comparable to Western benchmarks for most common procedures. The complication rate for a knee replacement at Bumrungrad is not materially different from one at a London teaching hospital.

The genuine risks are logistical, not clinical. Being far from home when something concerning happens. Having a GP at home who does not know your Bangkok records. Flying too soon after a procedure. Not having the right insurance. Choosing a hospital based on price rather than accreditation.

All of those risks are manageable. They are also exactly the things a local co-ordinator handles.
