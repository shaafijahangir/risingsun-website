Things can go wrong after surgery. This is true whether you have it in your home country or in Bangkok. The question is not whether risk exists. It is whether it is being managed correctly.

## What "Going Wrong" Actually Means

Complications exist on a spectrum.

**Minor complications** are common after any surgery, regardless of where it happens. Wound infection, swelling beyond the expected timeline, pain that exceeds the normal range, nausea from anaesthesia. These happen at NHS hospitals and at Bumrungrad. They are managed with antibiotics, adjusted pain protocols, or an extra night in hospital.

**Major complications** such as deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, or adverse anaesthetic reactions are rare. They are rare at home and rare in Bangkok. The difference is not the complication rate. It is who is managing it.

**Disappointing results** are a separate category. Not medical emergencies. Managed through revision consultations or time. Not unique to medical tourism.

## What Bangkok Hospitals Can Handle

JCI-accredited hospitals in Bangkok are tertiary care facilities equipped for complex and critical care. Bumrungrad operates a 24-hour, 60-bed ICU with fellowship-trained cardiologists. Samitivej has a dedicated surgical intensive care unit. Both have on-call anaesthesiologists, blood bank services, and 24-hour emergency capacity.

If something goes wrong during or immediately after surgery at these hospitals, the infrastructure matches a major London or Sydney teaching hospital. In some respects, staffing ratios and response times are faster, because these hospitals are privately run.

## What Happens After You Fly Home

This is where real complexity lies. You had a knee replacement in Bangkok. Six weeks later, back in Manchester, your wound looks red. Your GP did not perform the surgery. Your Bangkok surgeon is 9,000 kilometres away.

Here is what should happen, if you prepared correctly:

**Your GP has your discharge documentation.** JCI hospitals produce discharge summaries, operative notes, implant records, and post-operative protocols in English. With these, your GP can diagnose and treat a wound infection exactly as they would after any other surgery. I arrange this as standard for every patient. Without it, your GP is working blind.

**You have telemedicine access to your Bangkok surgeon.** Bumrungrad and Samitivej both offer post-operative video consultations, available within 24–48 hours for less than a private appointment at home. Most post-operative concerns are resolved in a single call.

**You have the right insurance.** Standard holiday insurance does not cover complications from a planned procedure. Medical travel insurance does: emergency treatment, repatriation if needed, and in some cases additional recovery costs. Not all providers cover planned procedures abroad. I can direct you to ones that do.

**You know your red flag symptoms.** Fever above 38.5°C, wound opening or discharge, sudden swelling, chest pain, difficulty breathing. These are not telemedicine situations. The threshold for calling emergency services does not change because you had surgery in Thailand.

## Why Cases Go Wrong

In my experience, the common factors are:

Patients who withheld medical history. Diabetes, blood clotting disorders, cardiac conditions all affect surgical risk. Surgeons ask these questions for a reason.

Flying too soon. DVT is a real risk on long-haul flights after major surgery. Typically 4–6 weeks after joint replacement, 2–3 weeks after abdominal surgery. I do not coordinate early departures without surgical clearance.

Choosing a hospital on price alone. The cost difference between accredited and non-accredited facilities reflects real differences in safety infrastructure. A clinic that saves you $3,000 with no ICU backup and no proper anaesthetist is not a saving.

Not planning the recovery period. Surgery is not the hard part. Patients without appropriate accommodation, physiotherapy access, and a stable routine have worse outcomes. In Bangkok, that planning is yours to arrange, not the health system's.

## The Checklist

Before you travel for any medical procedure:

- Get written discharge documentation: operative notes, implant records, post-operative protocol, in English
- Share those documents with your home GP before flying
- Confirm telemedicine access with your Bangkok hospital before discharge
- Buy medical travel insurance that explicitly covers planned procedures abroad
- Know the red flag symptoms for your specific procedure
- Book follow-up appointments at home before leaving Bangkok

At JCI-accredited hospitals in Bangkok, with proper preparation and appropriate insurance, the risk profile for standard elective procedures is comparable to equivalent surgery at home. The cost is 50–70% lower. The wait is weeks, not months.
