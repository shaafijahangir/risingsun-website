Nobody wants to think about this.

But the question — what if something goes wrong? — is the one underneath every other question about medical tourism. It surfaces as "is it safe?" or "do they speak English?" or "what does the follow-up look like?" but it is always the same underlying fear.

You deserve a straight answer. Not reassurance. An actual answer.

## First: What "Going Wrong" Usually Means

Medical complications exist on a spectrum. Understanding that spectrum matters before anything else.

**Minor complications** are common after any surgery, regardless of where it happens. Wound infection, swelling beyond the expected timeline, pain that exceeds the normal range, nausea from anaesthesia. These happen at NHS hospitals, at Johns Hopkins, and at Bumrungrad. They are managed with antibiotics, adjusted pain protocols, or an extra night in hospital.

**Major complications** — anastomotic leaks, deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, adverse anaesthetic reactions — are rare. They are rare at home and rare in Bangkok. The difference is not the complication rate. It is who is managing it and where you are when it happens.

**Results that disappoint** — an outcome that is technically correct but not what you hoped for — are a separate category. These are not medical emergencies. They are managed through revision consultations, further procedures, or time. They are also not unique to medical tourism.

JCI-accredited hospitals in Bangkok are tertiary care facilities. That means they are equipped for complex and critical care — not just elective procedures.

Bumrungrad operates a 24-hour, 60-bed ICU. Their cardiac ICU is staffed by cardiologists with international fellowship training. Samitivej has a dedicated surgical intensive care unit. Both hospitals have on-call anaesthesiologists, blood bank services, and emergency intervention capacity 24 hours a day.

If something goes wrong in the operating theatre or in the immediate post-operative period at these hospitals, the infrastructure to manage it is the same standard you would find at a major London or Sydney teaching hospital. The scenario people are actually imagining — "I collapse in my hotel room in Bangkok with no one who knows what to do" — is not how medical emergencies after planned surgery typically unfold. You are in hospital for the highest-risk period. You have daily wound checks. Your surgeon sees you before discharge.

## The Harder Question: What Happens After You Fly Home

This is where the genuine complexity lies, and where good preparation matters.

You have had a knee replacement in Bangkok. You have flown home to Manchester. Six weeks later, your wound looks red and feels warm. Your GP did not perform the surgery. Your Bangkok surgeon is 9,000 kilometres away. What happens?

Here is what should happen, if you prepared correctly:

**Your GP has your discharge documentation.** JCI-accredited Thai hospitals produce discharge summaries, operative notes, implant records, and post-operative protocols in English. These documents tell your GP exactly what was done, what medications you were on, and what to watch for. I arrange this as standard for every patient I coordinate. Without it, your GP is working blind. With it, they can diagnose and treat a wound infection exactly as they would after any other surgery.

**You have telemedicine access to your Bangkok surgeon.** Bumrungrad and Samitivej both offer post-operative video consultations. If something is worrying you and you want the person who performed your surgery to look at it, that appointment is available within 24–48 hours and costs a fraction of a private consultation at home. For most post-operative concerns — wound appearance, mobility questions, pain management — a video call resolves it immediately.

**You have the right travel insurance.** This is the practical safety net that most people skip or get wrong. Standard holiday insurance does not cover complications from a planned surgical procedure. Medical travel insurance — a specific product — does. It covers emergency treatment related to your procedure, repatriation if necessary, and in some cases, additional recovery costs. I can direct you to providers that cover planned procedures abroad; not all of them do.

**You know what "call immediately" versus "see your GP" looks like.** Your discharge instructions should include clear guidance on red flag symptoms: fever above 38.5°C, wound opening or discharge, sudden swelling, chest pain, difficulty breathing. If any of these appear, the answer is not telemedicine — it is emergency services in your home country. The threshold for calling 999 or 911 does not change because you had surgery in Thailand.

Surgical complication rates at JCI-accredited Thai hospitals are not publicly comparable to NHS trusts or US hospitals in a direct, apples-to-apples way, because the reporting frameworks differ. What is published:

The anecdotal horror stories that circulate online are almost invariably from non-accredited clinics, black-market procedures, or situations where patients withheld medical history from their surgeon. These are not outcomes at Bumrungrad or Samitivej.

## What Does Not Go Well (And Why)

Honesty requires acknowledging the cases that do not go smoothly. In my experience, the common factors are:

**Patients who did not disclose their full medical history.** Diabetes, blood clotting disorders, cardiac conditions — these all affect surgical risk. Surgeons at Thai hospitals ask the same questions as surgeons anywhere. Patients who withhold information face the same consequences they would at home, and sometimes worse because the surgeon could not plan for it.

**Flying too soon after surgery.** Deep vein thrombosis is a real risk on long-haul flights after major surgery. The timeline for safe travel varies by procedure — typically 4–6 weeks after joint replacement, 2–3 weeks after abdominal surgery. I give every patient specific guidance on this and I do not coordinate early departures without surgical clearance.

**Choosing a hospital based on price alone.** The difference in price between an accredited and a non-accredited facility reflects real differences in safety infrastructure. Saving $3,000 on a procedure by going to a cut-price clinic is not a saving if it means no ICU backup, no proper anaesthetist, and no post-operative monitoring.

**Not planning for the recovery period.** Surgery is not the hard part. Recovery is. Patients who do not arrange appropriate accommodation, physiotherapy access, and a stable routine for the weeks after discharge have worse outcomes. This is true in Birmingham as well as Bangkok — but in Bangkok, it is yours to plan, not the NHS's.

## The Practical Checklist

Before you travel for any medical procedure:

- Obtain written discharge documentation from your surgeon — operative notes, implant records, post-operative protocol — in English
- Share those documents with your home GP before you fly
- Confirm telemedicine access with your Bangkok hospital before discharge
- Purchase medical travel insurance that explicitly covers planned procedures abroad
- Know the red flag symptoms for your specific procedure
- Book your follow-up appointments at home before you leave Bangkok

None of this is complicated. All of it is what I coordinate for every patient I work with.
