Every Bangkok travel guide tells you the same things. The Grand Palace. Wat Pho. Street food on Khao San Road. Tuk-tuks and floating markets.

This is not that guide.

I have lived and worked in Bangkok since 1988. I have watched tens of thousands of travellers arrive in this city. I know which parts of the standard advice are accurate, which are outdated, and which were never true to begin with. Here is what I would tell a first-timer who wanted to actually understand the place.

## Bangkok Is Not One City

The single most useful thing to understand before you arrive is that Bangkok is not a single place. It is several completely different cities stacked on top of each other, connected by a rail system that does not cover all of them.

**Sukhumvit** is the expat corridor — international restaurants, good hospitals, shopping malls, and the BTS Skytrain running down the middle of it. This is where most international visitors base themselves, and with good reason. It is practical and well-connected.

**Silom and Sathorn** is the business district — suits in the morning, nightlife in the evening. Patpong is here, which tourists visit out of curiosity and usually find less interesting than expected.

**Old Bangkok (Rattanakosin)** is where the palaces and famous temples are. It is on the Chao Phraya river, 30–45 minutes from Sukhumvit by taxi, and feels like a different city. Worth at least a day.

**Ari and Samsen** are residential, local-feeling neighbourhoods with excellent neighbourhood restaurants and almost no tourists. If you want to feel like you are somewhere real, spend an afternoon here.

**Thonglor and Ekkamai** are upscale Bangkok — the places Bangkokians go for good food and evenings out. Less important for a first-time visitor but worth knowing about.

Understanding these neighbourhoods prevents the most common first-timer mistake: staying in one area and assuming you have seen Bangkok.

## The Transport Situation

Bangkok traffic is legendary for a reason. On a bad afternoon, a journey that takes 10 minutes in light traffic takes 45 minutes in a car.

The BTS Skytrain and MRT subway are fast, cheap, and air-conditioned. They do not cover everywhere — particularly Old Bangkok — but they cover Sukhumvit thoroughly. A day pass costs around 120 baht (approximately £2.80 / $3.50 USD). Use it for anything near a station.

For anywhere else, the options are:

**Grab (the regional version of Uber).** Fixed prices, no negotiation, reliable. This is how sensible Bangkok residents get around. Download the app before you land.

**Metered taxis.** There are many of them, they are cheap, and most are honest. The ones that are not honest will suggest they know a shortcut, refuse to use the meter, or tell you your destination is closed today. If a taxi driver does not turn on the meter immediately, get out and take the next one.

**Tuk-tuks.** Atmospheric and chaotic. Negotiable prices that always end up higher than a metered taxi for the same journey. Fine for a short tourist experience. Not efficient transport.

**Motorbike taxis.** The orange-vested drivers near most BTS stations. Very fast in traffic. Helmets are available but rarely offered. Not recommended if you are in Bangkok for medical reasons or if you have any hesitation about Bangkok road conditions.

## The Food Situation

The food in Bangkok is excellent. It is also not fully represented by what ends up in front of tourists on Khao San Road.

**Or Tor Kor Market** near Chatuchak is where Bangkok's premium fresh produce comes from — it is also a remarkable place to eat. Fruit, curries, grilled meats, and prepared Thai food of a quality that most restaurants cannot match. Go in the morning.

**Chatuchak Weekend Market** is enormous, overwhelming, and genuinely worth it on a Saturday or Sunday morning before the heat becomes unbearable. There is a separate section near JJ Mall with excellent Thai food stalls that tourists rarely find.

**The Sukhumvit street food corridor** — particularly around Soi 38 and the area around Thong Lo BTS — has reliable pad thai, boat noodles, and grilled meats that are frequented by Thai residents, not specifically tourists. The price difference from the tourist-facing versions is usually 30–50%.

**7-Eleven and Family Mart.** I say this without irony: Thai convenience stores are genuinely good. The food is fresh, cheap, and includes items — rice boxes, steamed buns, fruit cups — that are not the sad hot dog rollers of Western convenience stores. When you are hungry at midnight in Bangkok, a 7-Eleven is not a compromise.

**What to be cautious with:** Tap water is not safe to drink. Ice in restaurants with serious food operations is fine — it comes from treated sources. Street ice of uncertain provenance is a different question. In general: stick to bottled water, eat from busy stalls where turnover is high, and be appropriately cautious with raw shellfish on your first few days while your stomach adjusts.

## The Temple Rules

Bangkok's temples are magnificent and almost universally free to enter. Two practical notes:

**Dress appropriately.** This means covered shoulders and covered knees for both men and women. Not a suggestion — you will be turned away or given a sarong to borrow (which is fine, but saves time to come prepared). Light linen trousers and a loose shirt work in every temple in Bangkok.

**The Grand Palace requires more modest dress** than any other temple and has genuinely strict enforcement. Do not turn up in shorts and a vest expecting to sort it out at the door.

**Shoes come off** at temple entrances — at the chedi, not always at the grounds. If you see shoes at a threshold, your shoes come off.

## The One Thing Most Tourists Skip

The Chao Phraya river.

Not the tourist boat. Not the Mandarin Oriental's bar at sunset (though that is excellent). The river itself, experienced at street level.

Take the Chao Phraya Express Boat — 15–16 baht (approximately 40p) — from Sathorn Pier and ride it north to Bangkrachao or south to Wat Arun. It is Bangkok's most honest cross-section: commuters, monks, schoolchildren, market traders, tourists from everywhere, all on the same ancient wooden boat. The skyline from the water is different from any photograph. Get off at a pier you do not recognise and walk ten minutes in any direction.

You will find something that is not in any guide.

## What Bangkok Rewards

Bangkok rewards curiosity and punishes a fixed itinerary. The best experiences here are the ones you did not plan — the restaurant you found by following your nose down an alley, the temple you walked into on the way to somewhere else, the conversation that started with directions and ended somewhere completely unexpected.

The city is overwhelming for the first 24 hours, manageable on day two, and genuinely engaging by day three. Give it enough time.
