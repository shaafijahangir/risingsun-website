Bangkok is not one city. It is several completely different places stacked on top of each other, connected by a rail system that does not cover all of them. Understanding this before you arrive prevents the most common first-timer mistake: staying in one area and assuming you have seen Bangkok.

## The Neighbourhoods That Matter

**Sukhumvit** is where most international visitors base themselves, and with good reason. International restaurants, good hospitals, shopping malls, and the BTS Skytrain running straight through it. Practical and well-connected.

**Silom and Sathorn** is the business district: suits in the morning, nightlife in the evening.

**Old Bangkok (Rattanakosin)** is where the palaces and famous temples are. It sits on the Chao Phraya river, 30-45 minutes from Sukhumvit by taxi. It feels like a different city. Worth at least a full day.

**Ari and Samsen** are residential, local-feeling neighbourhoods with excellent food and almost no tourists. If you want to feel like you are somewhere real, spend an afternoon here.

**Thonglor and Ekkamai** are where Bangkokians go for good food and evenings out.

## Getting Around

Bangkok traffic is legendary for a reason. A 10-minute journey in light traffic takes 45 minutes on a bad afternoon.

The BTS Skytrain and MRT subway are fast, cheap, and air-conditioned. A day pass costs around 120 baht (approximately £2.80 / $3.50). Use them for anything near a station.

**Grab** is the regional version of Uber. Fixed prices, no negotiation, reliable. Download the app before you land.

**Metered taxis** are cheap and mostly honest. If a driver does not turn on the meter immediately, get out and take the next one.

**Tuk-tuks** are atmospheric. They also cost more than a metered taxi for the same journey. Fine for one tourist experience, not efficient transport.

**Motorbike taxis** (orange vests near BTS stations) are fast in traffic. Not recommended if you have any hesitation about Bangkok road conditions.

## Food

The food in Bangkok is excellent. It is not fully represented by what ends up in front of tourists on Khao San Road.

**Or Tor Kor Market** near Chatuchak is where Bangkok's premium fresh produce comes from. Go in the morning for curries, grilled meats, and prepared Thai food of a quality most restaurants cannot match.

**Chatuchak Weekend Market** is enormous and worth it on a Saturday or Sunday morning before the heat becomes unbearable. The food section near JJ Mall is excellent and largely tourist-free.

**Sukhumvit street stalls** around Soi 38 and Thong Lo are frequented by Thai residents. Prices run 30-50% lower than tourist-facing versions of the same dishes.

**7-Eleven and Family Mart.** Thai convenience stores are genuinely good. Fresh rice boxes, steamed buns, fruit cups. Not a compromise when you are hungry at midnight.

Tap water is not safe to drink. Ice at established restaurants is fine. Stick to bottled water, eat from busy stalls with high turnover, and be cautious with raw shellfish for the first few days.

## Temple Rules

Bangkok's temples are magnificent and almost universally free to enter.

Covered shoulders and covered knees are required for both men and women. You will be turned away without them. Light linen trousers and a loose shirt work everywhere.

The Grand Palace has stricter enforcement than any other temple. Do not arrive in shorts expecting to sort it out at the door.

Shoes come off at temple entrances. If you see shoes at a threshold, your shoes come off.

## The Thing Most Tourists Skip

The Chao Phraya river.

Take the Chao Phraya Express Boat (15-16 baht) from Sathorn Pier north or south along the river. Commuters, monks, schoolchildren, market traders, tourists from everywhere: all on the same ancient wooden boats. The skyline from the water looks different from any photograph. Get off at a pier you do not recognise and walk ten minutes in any direction.

You will find something that is not in any guide.

## What Bangkok Rewards

Bangkok rewards curiosity and punishes a fixed itinerary. The best experiences here are the ones you did not plan. The city is overwhelming for the first 24 hours, manageable on day two, and genuinely engaging by day three. Give it enough time.
