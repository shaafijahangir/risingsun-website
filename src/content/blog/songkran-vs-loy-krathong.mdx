Thailand has two festivals worth changing your travel dates for. They could not be more different.

One is a three-day water fight that turns Bangkok and Chiang Mai into a joyful, chaotic river. The other is a single evening of extraordinary quiet beauty: thousands of lanterns and floating candles released over dark water.

Here is how to choose between them.

## Songkran: Thai New Year (April 13 to 15)

Songkran is Thai New Year. The traditional observance involves sprinkling water as a gesture of blessing. In modern Bangkok and Chiang Mai, it has become something more elemental: a national water fight.

On the main Songkran streets (Silom Road in Bangkok and the Old City moat road in Chiang Mai), people line the streets with buckets and water guns. Trucks move slowly with barrels of water. Locals, tourists, and children are all equally soaked. There is no shelter. There is no staying dry. You either participate or you stay indoors.

The mood is genuinely euphoric. Songkran is the one time of year when everyone, regardless of age or background, is doing the same thing: laughing and throwing water at strangers. The social barriers of normal Thai public life dissolve for three days.

**Chiang Mai vs Bangkok for Songkran**

Chiang Mai is the better experience. The Old City moat becomes the epicentre: a 7km ring road around the ancient walled city, where the water fight runs continuously for three days. The scale is intimate enough to feel participatory rather than overwhelming.

Bangkok's Songkran is larger and more intense. Silom Road and Khao San Road are the main spots. Extraordinary, but more carnival-commercial and less culturally centred.

**Practical notes:**

- Protect your phone. A waterproof case is essential. I have watched expensive phones die on day one.
- Leave valuables at your accommodation. Whatever you carry will get wet.
- Book accommodation 2 to 3 months ahead. Hotel prices approximately double.
- Expect road closures and impossible traffic.
- Festivities often start April 12, peak April 13 to 15.

**Best for:** People who want to immerse themselves in a local experience, are comfortable with noise and chaos, and want a story to tell. Families with older children find the joy infectious.

**Not ideal for:** Anyone who needs to be somewhere on time, is post-surgery, or is travelling for business during this period.

## Loy Krathong: Festival of Lights (November, Full Moon)

Loy Krathong falls on the full moon of the 12th Thai lunar month, typically November. The festival involves releasing small floats made of banana leaves and flowers onto rivers and lakes, each carrying a candle and incense.

In Chiang Mai, Loy Krathong coincides with Yi Peng, the northern Thai sky lantern festival. Thousands of paper lanterns are released into the night sky simultaneously. This is the image you have seen in photographs: a dark sky slowly filling with warm light, the river below glittering with floating candles.

The scale of Yi Peng is difficult to describe to someone who has not seen it. In the moments when the mass lantern releases happen, the sky genuinely transforms. The night becomes warm and luminous. It is one of the most beautiful things I have seen in twenty years in Thailand.

The mood is the opposite of Songkran. Where Songkran is noise and chaos, Loy Krathong and Yi Peng are quiet, contemplative, and visually extraordinary. People make wishes as they release their krathong. It is a festival about letting go of the past year's troubles.

**Bangkok vs Chiang Mai for Loy Krathong**

Chiang Mai wins clearly, because of Yi Peng. The sky lantern element transforms the festival from a lovely riverside event into something genuinely unforgettable.

Bangkok's Loy Krathong on the Chao Phraya is beautiful. The river carries thousands of floating candles past the temple complexes. But Bangkok lacks the lantern sky.

**Practical notes:**

- Chiang Mai flights and accommodation sell out well in advance for Yi Peng. Book 2 to 3 months ahead.
- The date shifts each year with the lunar calendar. Check the specific date before planning.
- Large-scale organised lantern events are managed carefully for fire safety. Smaller spontaneous releases happen throughout the old city.
- Build extra time for any journey into Chiang Mai that evening. Traffic is significant.

**Best for:** Anyone who values beauty and cultural depth. Couples, photographers, and anyone who wants an experience they will remember for the rest of their life.
