Thailand has two festivals that are worth changing your travel dates for.

They could not be more different. One is a three-day water fight that turns the streets of Bangkok and Chiang Mai into a joyful, chaotic river. The other is a single evening of extraordinary quiet beauty — thousands of paper lanterns and floating flowers released over dark water.

Both are worth experiencing. Here is how to choose between them.

## Songkran — Thai New Year (April 13–15)

Songkran is Thai New Year, celebrated across the country from April 13th to 15th. The traditional observance involves sprinkling water as a gesture of blessing and respect. In modern Bangkok and Chiang Mai, it has become something more elemental: a national water fight.

**What it actually looks like:**

On the main Songkran streets — Silom Road in Bangkok, the Old City moat road in Chiang Mai — the experience is total. People line the streets with buckets and water guns. Trucks move slowly with barrels of water. Locals, tourists, monks (mostly elsewhere), and children are all equally soaked. There is no shelter. There is no staying dry. You either participate or you stay indoors.

The mood is genuinely euphoric. Songkran is the one time of year when everyone, regardless of age or background, is doing the same thing at the same time, and that thing is laughing and throwing water at strangers. The social barriers that normally operate in Thai public life dissolve for three days.

**Chiang Mai vs Bangkok for Songkran:**

Chiang Mai is consistently rated the better Songkran experience. The Old City moat becomes the epicentre — a 7km ring road around the ancient walled city, where the water fight runs continuously for three days. The scale is intimate enough to feel participatory rather than overwhelming.

Bangkok's Songkran is larger and more intense. Silom Road and Khao San Road are the main battlegrounds. It is extraordinary but can feel more carnival-commercial and less culturally centred.

**Practical considerations:**

- Protect your phone. A waterproof case is essential. I have watched expensive phones die on day one.
- Leave valuables at your accommodation. Whatever you carry will get wet.
- Book accommodation 2–3 months in advance. Songkran is high demand. Hotel prices approximately double.
- Expect transport disruption. Major roads close and traffic is genuinely impossible.
- The celebration is loudest April 13–15, but festivities often start the 12th.

**Best for:** People who want to immerse themselves in a genuinely local experience, are comfortable with chaos and noise, and want a story to tell. Also remarkable for families with older children — the joy is infectious.

**Not ideal for:** Anyone who needs to be somewhere on time, is post-surgery or has any physical limitation that makes getting soaked and jostled problematic, or is travelling for business purposes during this period.

## Loy Krathong — Festival of Lights (November, Full Moon)

Loy Krathong falls on the full moon of the 12th month in the Thai lunar calendar — typically November. The festival involves releasing small floats (krathong) made of banana leaves and flowers onto rivers, canals, and lakes, each carrying a candle, incense, and a small offering.

In Chiang Mai, Loy Krathong coincides with Yi Peng — the northern Thai sky lantern festival, where thousands of paper lanterns (khom loi) are released into the night sky simultaneously. This is the image you have seen in photographs: a dark sky slowly filling with warm light rising upward, the river below glittering with floating candles.

**What it actually looks like:**

The scale of Yi Peng in Chiang Mai is difficult to describe to someone who has not seen it. In the moments when the mass lantern releases happen — there are organised events, and spontaneous releases throughout the evening — the sky genuinely transforms. The night becomes warm and luminous. It is one of the most beautiful things I have seen in twenty years in Thailand.

The mood is the opposite of Songkran. Where Songkran is noise and chaos and laughter, Loy Krathong and Yi Peng are quiet, contemplative, and visually extraordinary. People make wishes as they release their krathong. It is a festival about letting go of the past year's troubles and bad luck.

**Bangkok vs Chiang Mai for Loy Krathong:**

Chiang Mai is the clear winner, specifically because of Yi Peng. The sky lantern element transforms Loy Krathong from a lovely riverside event into something genuinely unforgettable.

Bangkok's Loy Krathong is beautiful on the Chao Phraya — the river carries thousands of floating candles past the temple complexes, and the reflection on the water is extraordinary. But Bangkok lacks the lantern sky of Chiang Mai.

**Practical considerations:**

- Chiang Mai flights and accommodation sell out significantly in advance for Yi Peng. Book early — ideally 2–3 months ahead.
- The date moves each year with the lunar calendar. Check the specific date for your year before planning.
- Yi Peng sky lanterns are fire hazards. Large-scale organised events now require permission and are managed carefully. Smaller spontaneous releases happen throughout the old city.
- Traffic on the main roads into Chiang Mai during Yi Peng is significant. Build extra time for any journey.

**Best for:** Anyone who values beauty and cultural depth over spectacle. Couples, photographers, spiritual travellers, and anyone who wants an experience they will remember for the rest of their life.

**Not ideal for:** Travellers who need to stay dry from Yi Peng smoke (the sky can get hazy with lantern ash), or those who cannot tolerate crowds in a contained area.

## If You Can Only Choose One

If you are the kind of traveller who wants to feel like you are inside a foreign culture rather than watching it — Songkran.

If you are the kind of traveller who wants to witness something that has no equivalent anywhere else in the world — Yi Peng.

Both are worth the trip. If you have any flexibility in your dates, building your Thailand itinerary around one of them transforms the trip from a holiday to an experience.
