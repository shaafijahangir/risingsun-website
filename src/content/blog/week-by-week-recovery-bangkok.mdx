Most people spend weeks researching the surgery.

Very few spend time thinking about what happens after the surgeon closes and the real work begins.

Recovery is not glamorous, and it is not what travel blogs write about. But it is what determines whether your experience in Bangkok is positive or miserable — and, more importantly, whether your long-term outcome is good.

Here is what recovery from major surgery in Bangkok actually looks like, week by week. I am using joint replacement as the reference case because it is the most common complex procedure I coordinate, but the principles apply to most elective surgery.

## Before You Arrive: The Planning That Makes Everything Easier

The decisions you make before surgery shape everything that comes after.

**Book your post-discharge accommodation before you fly.** You cannot sort this while you are in a hospital bed. You need somewhere with elevator access, a bed at a comfortable height (not a floor-level Thai-style mattress), easy bathroom access, and ideally a kitchenette or room service. A serviced apartment in Sukhumvit — within 15 minutes of Bumrungrad or Samitivej — is the standard recommendation.

**Bring loose, easy-to-fasten clothing.** Post-operative swelling is real. Tight waistbands and stiff shoes become obstacles. Pack elasticated trousers, slip-on shoes, and loose tops. You will not be dressing for anything else for the first two weeks.

**Tell someone at home your schedule.** Not so they worry — so that someone knows where you are, what hospital you are in, and when to expect to hear from you. Set a simple daily check-in routine.

## Week 1: The Hospital

You will be in hospital for 4–6 nights after joint replacement (2–4 nights after most abdominal procedures, 1–2 nights after simpler operations). This is where the most intense part of recovery happens, and where the clinical team earns their fee.

**Days 1–2:** The first 48 hours are the hardest. Anaesthetic wears off gradually. Pain is managed with a nerve block (for joint surgery), epidural analgesics, and oral medication. You will feel groggy, possibly nauseous, and generally not inclined to do much. This is appropriate. Your job is to sleep, stay hydrated, and let the nursing staff manage the rest.

At Bumrungrad and Samitivej, nursing staff check in every 1–2 hours. The rooms are private, quiet, and well-maintained. The food is served from a proper menu with international options — not hospital tray food as Westerners typically experience it. One family member or companion can usually stay overnight.

**Days 3–4:** Physiotherapy begins. For knee replacement, this means getting to standing and taking a few supervised steps with a frame. This is uncomfortable. Do it anyway. Early mobilisation reduces DVT risk, reduces muscle atrophy, and determines how quickly you regain function. Your physiotherapist has seen this thousands of times. Trust the process.

Pain is real on these days. The combination of nerve block wearing off and the effort of early physio is the most demanding part of the entire recovery. Take the pain medication as prescribed, on schedule — do not wait until you are in significant pain before taking it.

**Days 5–6:** Wound checks, possible drain removal (for some procedures), continued physiotherapy. Most patients are managing 20–30 steps with a frame or crutches by discharge. The clinical team will confirm you are ready for discharge when your wound is stable, your pain is manageable on oral medication, and you can transfer in and out of a bed safely.

## Week 2: The Transition

You are out of hospital and into your serviced apartment. This is the week most people underestimate.

You are not well. You are not on holiday. You are mobile enough to move between the bed, bathroom, and a chair. You are tired in a way that sleep does not fully fix. Your appetite is reduced. You may have moments of wondering why you did this.

This is completely normal and temporary.

**What to focus on:** Following your prescribed exercises. These are not optional — they are what prevents your new joint from stiffening during the healing period. Your Bangkok physiotherapist will come to you (or you will go to them — I arrange transport for every client) for daily or every-other-day sessions. These typically last 45–60 minutes and focus on range of motion, strength, and safe movement patterns.

**What to eat:** Bangkok has exceptional food delivery infrastructure. Hospital-grade nutrition is not the goal — adequate protein, hydration, and avoiding alcohol is. Most serviced apartments in Sukhumvit have easy access to restaurants, delivery apps, and supermarkets with familiar products.

**What to do with your mind:** The confinement of week 2 is psychologically harder than the surgery for many patients. Have a plan: films, books, podcasts, video calls with family. The serviced apartment pools in this area of Bangkok are accessible with appropriate caution — floating in warm water is one of the better things you can do for both swelling reduction and mood.

**Pain in week 2:** Should be meaningfully better than week 1, and manageable on oral analgesics alone. If it is not improving, call your surgeon. This is not a question for the internet — it is a question for the hospital.

## Week 3: The Turn

By week 3, most patients with standard recoveries begin to feel like themselves again.

Walking distances increase from "across the room" to "around the block." The physiotherapy sessions begin introducing more ambitious exercises. Swelling is still present but noticeably reduced. Appetite returns. Sleep improves.

This is the week when Bangkok starts to become enjoyable rather than just endured.

**Gentle outings** — a slow walk to a nearby café, a visit to a temple market, a taxi ride to a riverside restaurant — are reasonable by week 3 for most joint replacement patients. Not a full day of sightseeing. Not a standing cooking class. A few hours of gentle activity with frequent rest.

**The flight home question** is answered in week 3. For most patients, the answer is: not yet. Four weeks is the standard minimum for long-haul flights after joint replacement. Six weeks is more comfortable. Your surgeon will assess your wound, your mobility, and your DVT risk at your week-3 post-operative consultation and give you specific clearance.

## Week 4 and Beyond: Going Home

By week 4, you are ready — or nearly ready — to travel home. The wound is healed. The swelling is manageable. The range of motion is developing. You can walk meaningful distances, manage steps, and survive a long-haul flight with appropriate precautions (aisle seat, compression stockings, regular movement, hydration).

The Bangkok chapter ends here. The home chapter continues.

**Before you leave:** Take every document your hospital has issued. Discharge summary, operative notes, implant records, physiotherapy notes, post-operative protocol. Your home GP needs all of it. Your home physiotherapist needs the exercise protocol to continue seamlessly. Your home orthopaedic surgeon (if you have one for follow-up) needs the operative notes.

I arrange a final briefing pack for every client — a clear summary of the Bangkok treatment, what the home team needs to know, and the ongoing monitoring schedule.

**At home:** Recovery continues for 3–6 months. The Bangkok chapter ends, but the physiotherapy does not. Most patients need 2–3 months of home physiotherapy to reach full functional recovery after joint replacement. This is covered by most health systems as part of standard post-surgical care, regardless of where the surgery took place.

The outcome at 12 months is what matters. And the research is consistent: for standard elective procedures at accredited facilities, outcomes at 12 months are not statistically different based on whether the surgery happened in Bangkok or Birmingham.

## One Thing Most People Do Not Plan For

The emotional component of recovery — the frustration of slower progress than expected, the isolation of the early weeks, the anxiety about whether everything is healing correctly — is real and worth acknowledging.

It helps to have someone in your corner who you can call with the questions that do not feel big enough for the hospital but are consuming your energy. That is part of what I do — not just logistics, but a reliable voice during the recovery period when uncertainty is highest.
