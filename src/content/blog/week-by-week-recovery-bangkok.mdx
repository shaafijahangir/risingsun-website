Most people research the surgery for weeks. Almost nobody researches what comes after.

Recovery is what determines whether your Bangkok experience is positive or miserable. It also determines your long-term outcome. Here is what it actually looks like, week by week. I am using joint replacement as the reference case because it is the most common complex procedure I coordinate, but the structure applies to most elective surgery.

## Before You Arrive

The decisions you make before surgery shape everything after.

Book your post-discharge accommodation before you fly. You cannot sort this from a hospital bed. You need elevator access, a bed at a comfortable height, easy bathroom access, and ideally a kitchenette. A serviced apartment in Sukhumvit, within 15 minutes of Bumrungrad or Samitivej, is the standard recommendation.

Pack loose clothing. Post-operative swelling is real. Bring elasticated trousers, slip-on shoes, and loose tops. Tell someone at home your hospital name, surgeon name, and flight details. Set a daily check-in time.

## Week 1: The Hospital

Joint replacement means 4-6 nights in hospital. Most abdominal procedures are 2-4 nights. This is where the acute phase happens.

Days 1-2 are the hardest. Anaesthetic wears off gradually. Pain is managed with a nerve block, epidural analgesics, and oral medication. You will feel groggy and not inclined to do much. Your job is to sleep, stay hydrated, and let the nursing staff manage the rest. At Bumrungrad and Samitivej, nurses check in every 1-2 hours. Rooms are private and quiet. The food is ordered from a proper menu, not a tray. One companion can usually stay overnight.

Days 3-4: physiotherapy begins. For knee replacement, this means getting to standing and taking supervised steps with a frame. It is uncomfortable. Do it anyway. Early mobilisation reduces DVT risk and determines how quickly you regain function. Take pain medication on schedule. Do not wait until you are in significant pain.

Days 5-6: wound checks, drain removal for some procedures, continued physio. Most patients manage 20-30 steps with a frame by discharge. The team confirms you are ready when your wound is stable, pain is manageable on oral medication, and you can transfer in and out of bed safely.

## Week 2: The Transition

You are in your serviced apartment. This is the week people underestimate.

You are not well. You are mobile enough to move between the bed, bathroom, and a chair. You are tired in a way sleep does not fix. Your appetite is reduced.

This is normal and temporary.

Follow your prescribed exercises. They prevent the joint from stiffening during healing. Your Bangkok physiotherapist visits daily or every other day. I arrange transport for every client. Sessions run 45-60 minutes and focus on range of motion, strength, and safe movement patterns.

Bangkok has excellent food delivery infrastructure. Adequate protein, hydration, and no alcohol is the goal. The serviced apartment pools in Sukhumvit are accessible with care. Floating in warm water helps with swelling and mood.

Pain in week 2 should be meaningfully better than week 1 and manageable on oral analgesics. If it is not improving, call your surgeon.

## Week 3: The Turn

By week 3, most patients start feeling like themselves again.

Walking distances increase from across the room to around the block. Physio introduces more ambitious exercises. Swelling reduces. Appetite returns.

Gentle outings are possible for most joint replacement patients: a slow walk to a nearby café, a taxi to a riverside restaurant, a temple visit. Not a full day of sightseeing. A few hours with frequent rest.

The flight home question is answered here. For most patients, the answer is: not yet. Four weeks is the standard minimum for long-haul flights after joint replacement. Six weeks is more comfortable. Your surgeon will assess you at the week-3 consultation and give specific clearance.

## Week 4: Going Home

The wound is healed. Swelling is manageable. You can walk meaningful distances, manage steps, and survive a long-haul flight with precautions: aisle seat, compression stockings, regular movement, hydration.

Before you leave, take every document the hospital issued. Discharge summary, operative notes, implant records, physiotherapy notes, post-operative protocol. Your home GP, home physiotherapist, and any home specialist needs all of it. I arrange a final briefing pack for every client covering what the home team needs to know.

Recovery continues for 3-6 months after you land. Most patients need 2-3 months of home physiotherapy to reach full function after joint replacement. At 12 months, outcomes at accredited Bangkok hospitals are not statistically different from equivalent surgery anywhere else.
