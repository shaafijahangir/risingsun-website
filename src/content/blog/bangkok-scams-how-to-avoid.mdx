The scams in Bangkok are not sophisticated.

They have not changed meaningfully in thirty years. The same five or six scenarios play out on the same streets, targeting the same category of tourist, with the same outcomes. They work not because they are clever but because first-time visitors to Bangkok have no reason to know about them.

After twenty years of watching this happen — to visitors I was helping and to visitors I was not — I can tell you every one of them, how each works, and exactly what to say.

## The Tuk-Tuk Gem Scam (The Classic)

**How it works:** A friendly local approaches you near a tourist attraction — often the Grand Palace, Wat Pho, or the Democracy Monument. He tells you the attraction is closed today (national holiday, royal ceremony, cleaning day — it varies). He offers to take you somewhere else in his tuk-tuk, perhaps a famous temple you have not heard of, and proposes a very low fare.

The tuk-tuk tour involves one or two brief stops and then an extended visit to a gem shop. You are shown beautiful gems at "factory prices" — rubies, sapphires, emeralds. The driver waits outside and will take you home when you are done. The gems turn out to be synthetic or low-grade, and worth a fraction of what you paid. Getting a refund is essentially impossible.

**What to do:** The attraction is not closed. It has not been closed in twenty years for the reasons described. If anyone on the street near a major temple tells you an attraction is closed today, smile and walk directly to the entrance. The tuk-tuk driver's livelihood depends on commission from the gem shop, not from the fare.

If you want to buy gems in Bangkok, buy from established retailers or from a hotel jewellery shop — not from anywhere introduced via a tuk-tuk.

## The Taxi No-Meter Situation

**How it works:** You get into a taxi and the driver does not turn on the meter. Instead, he quotes you a price for the journey — almost always 2–4× what the metered fare would be.

This happens most often at tourist locations (Khao San Road, major temples, the airport pickup lane), at night, and when there is a language barrier. The driver is betting you do not know what the journey should cost.

**What to do:** Before you get into the taxi, check that the driver turns on the meter. If he does not do so immediately or says the meter is broken, get out and take the next one. Metered taxis in Bangkok are everywhere and they are cheap — do not negotiate when metered is available.

At Suvarnabhumi Airport, use the official metered taxi queue at Arrivals Level 1. Avoid anyone approaching you in the terminal offering a "private car" — these are consistently significantly more expensive.

Grab (the regional ride-hailing app) shows you the fare before you book and requires no negotiation. For late-night travel or airport journeys, it is the lowest-friction option.

## The Temple Redirect

**How it works:** A well-dressed, friendly man near a major temple engages you in conversation and learns that you are visiting the Grand Palace or Wat Pho. He helpfully informs you that the attraction does not open for another two hours. In the meantime, he knows a smaller temple that is much more beautiful and far less crowded. He will show you the way.

The smaller temple is real but unremarkable. Along the way, there is a tailor, a travel agency, a souvenir shop, or all three. Your friendly guide receives commission for every visitor he delivers.

**What to do:** Check opening times before you leave your hotel. The Grand Palace opens at 8:30am and closes at 3:30pm. Wat Pho is open 8:00am–6:30pm. If anyone near these temples tells you otherwise, this is the opening line of the redirect.

## The Tailor Scam

**How it works:** A man approaches you and recommends a tailor for made-to-measure suits or dresses. The suits are quick — one to two days — and the prices sound competitive. You pay, you return for fitting, you take home clothing that is poorly made, uses inferior fabric, and looks nothing like the sample you chose from.

Legitimate tailoring in Bangkok exists and is excellent. It is not introduced to you by a stranger on the street.

**What to do:** If you want made-to-measure clothing in Bangkok, research tailors independently before you travel. There are established shops in Sukhumvit and Silom with decades of history and honest reviews. Expect 3–5 days minimum for quality work, not overnight. Budget $150–400 for a quality suit, not $60.

## The Inflated Songthaew Fare on the Islands

**How it works:** On Koh Samui, Phuket, and Koh Phangan, songthaews (shared pick-up trucks) have fixed route fares — typically 50–100 baht for a standard journey. For tourists, drivers sometimes quote 300–500 baht, either hoping the tourist does not know the correct fare or waiting for a private hire rate.

**What to do:** Ask other passengers what they paid, or ask your hotel reception what the correct fare to your destination is. The standard fares are publicly known. If a driver quotes you significantly more, another songthaew will be along shortly.

## The Airport Taxi Scam (Specific to Suvarnabhumi)

**How it works:** As you exit arrivals at Bangkok's main international airport, men approach you offering private transport to your hotel. The prices quoted are 3–5× the metered taxi rate. Some offer to take you in a private car, which sounds preferable after a long flight.

**What to do:** Turn down everyone who approaches you in the arrivals hall. Go to the official Public Taxi queue at Arrivals Level 1 — it is clearly signed and metered. Add 50 baht for the expressway toll if you are going to central Bangkok. The total for a central Bangkok hotel should be 250–350 baht. Compare this to the 1,200–1,500 baht some visitors pay for the same journey from a "private transfer" arranged in the terminal.

If you prefer a pre-arranged transfer, book it through your hotel or through a verified service before you land. I arrange airport pickups for all clients as part of trip coordination — it costs slightly more than a metered taxi and eliminates the arrival uncertainty entirely.

## The Broader Principle

None of these scams succeed against someone who expects them. The investment is knowing they exist.

Bangkok is an extraordinarily safe city for tourists in the ways that matter most. Violent crime against tourists is rare. Petty theft is less common than in most European capitals. The scams above are financial inconveniences, not physical risks.

They are also entirely avoidable. The combination of knowing what to expect and having a local contact to call when something feels off removes virtually all of the risk.

When you are travelling with a plan that someone who knows Bangkok built for you — with accommodation, transport, and activity choices made based on actual knowledge — the scenario in which you are standing outside a closed temple being offered a tuk-tuk ride does not arise. You already know the temple is open, you already have transport booked, and you already know where you are going next.

That is the practical value of twenty years of local knowledge. It is not just the good things it unlocks. It is the bad things it prevents.
