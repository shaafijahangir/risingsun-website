Bangkok's scams have not changed in thirty years. The same five scenarios play out on the same streets, targeting the same tourists. They work because first-time visitors have no reason to know about them. Here they are.

## The Tuk-Tuk Gem Scam

A friendly local near the Grand Palace or Wat Pho tells you the attraction is closed today. National holiday, royal ceremony, cleaning day: the reason varies. He offers a tuk-tuk ride to a lesser-known temple at a very low fare.

The tour involves one or two brief stops, then an extended visit to a gem shop. You are shown rubies, sapphires, and emeralds at "factory prices." The gems are synthetic or low-grade, worth a fraction of what you paid. Getting a refund is not possible.

The attraction is not closed. It has not been closed for those reasons in twenty years. Walk directly to the entrance. The driver's income comes from gem shop commissions, not from your fare.

If you want gems in Bangkok, buy from established retailers with genuine reviews. Not from anywhere a tuk-tuk driver introduces you to.

## The Taxi No-Meter Situation

You get in, the driver does not start the meter. He quotes a price: almost always two to four times the metered fare.

This happens most at tourist spots, at night, and where there is a language barrier.

Before you get in, watch that the driver turns on the meter. If he says it is broken, get out. Metered taxis are everywhere and cheap.

At Suvarnabhumi Airport, use the official metered taxi queue at Arrivals Level 1. Avoid anyone in the terminal offering a private car.

## The Temple Redirect

A well-dressed man near the Grand Palace learns you are heading there. He tells you it does not open for another two hours. He knows a smaller temple nearby, far less crowded, and will show you the way.

The smaller temple is real but unremarkable. Along the route: a tailor, a travel agency, a souvenir shop, or all three. He collects commission for every visitor he delivers.

Check opening times before you leave your hotel. The Grand Palace opens at 8:30am. Wat Pho is open from 8:00am. If anyone nearby tells you otherwise, that is the opening line of the redirect.

## The Tailor Scam

A man recommends a tailor for made-to-measure suits. Quick turnaround, competitive prices. You return for your fitting and collect clothing that is poorly made, uses inferior fabric, and looks nothing like the sample.

Legitimate tailoring in Bangkok is excellent. It is not introduced to you by a stranger on the street.

Research tailors before you travel. The established shops in Sukhumvit and Silom have decades of history and honest reviews. Quality work takes 3 to 5 days minimum, not overnight. Budget 150 to 400 USD for a quality suit, not 60.

## The Inflated Songthaew Fare

On Koh Samui, Phuket, and Koh Phangan, songthaews have fixed route fares: typically 50 to 100 baht. For tourists, drivers sometimes quote 300 to 500 baht, betting you do not know the correct price.

Ask your hotel what the fare to your destination should be. Another songthaew will be along shortly if the quoted price is wrong.

## The Airport Private Transfer

You exit arrivals at Suvarnabhumi and men approach with offers of private transport. The prices are three to five times the metered taxi rate.

Turn down everyone who approaches you in the arrivals hall. Go to the official Public Taxi queue at Arrivals Level 1. It is clearly signed and metered. Add 50 baht for the expressway toll to central Bangkok. The total should be 250 to 350 baht. Some visitors pay 1,200 to 1,500 baht for the same journey from a "private transfer" arranged in the terminal.

## The Broader Picture

None of these scams succeed against someone who expects them.

Bangkok is safe for tourists in the ways that matter most. Violent crime against tourists is rare. Petty theft is less common than in most European capitals. The scenarios above are financial inconveniences, not physical risks.
