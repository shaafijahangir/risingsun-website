The standard Thailand itinerary goes: fly into Bangkok, spend two or three days, fly north to Chiang Mai, spend two or three days, fly south to a beach.

It works. It is also a template built for people who have not thought about what they actually want from the trip.

After twenty years of building itineraries for people visiting Thailand, the most common mistake I see is not choosing the wrong city — it is choosing based on what the travel guides say rather than what fits the person asking.

Here is how to actually make this decision.

## What Bangkok Is

Bangkok is a city of 10 million people. It is chaotic, dense, fast, and relentlessly interesting. The food is extraordinary and available at every price point. The nightlife is world-class. The shopping is excellent. The temples are some of the finest in Southeast Asia.

It is also hot, humid, loud, and exhausting if you are not built for sensory intensity. The traffic is genuinely bad. The distances between things are longer than a map suggests. You can spend two hours in a taxi in Bangkok and travel four kilometres.

Bangkok rewards people who lean into it. It exhausts people who resist it.

**Bangkok is right for you if:**
- You like cities and feel energised by them
- You are combining your trip with hospital or medical appointments (Bangkok is the hub for all serious medical tourism)
- You want world-class restaurants, rooftop bars, and nightlife
- You are comfortable navigating a complex transit system or using Grab heavily
- You have limited time and want maximum density of experience

## What Chiang Mai Is

Chiang Mai is Thailand's second city in name and reputation, but it feels nothing like a second city. The old town — surrounded by a square moat — is walkable, shaded, and quiet in a way that Bangkok never is. The pace is fundamentally different.

The food culture is distinct from Bangkok: northern Thai cuisine (khao soi, sai oua, naem) is among the most interesting regional food in Southeast Asia, and Chiang Mai is where you eat it properly. The surrounding landscape — forested mountains, hill tribe villages, elephant sanctuaries — is the only place in Thailand where the natural environment is the primary draw.

Chiang Mai is also significantly cheaper than Bangkok. Accommodation, food, and activities cost 20–40% less.

**Chiang Mai is right for you if:**
- You find large cities overwhelming or exhausting
- You want a slower, more reflective travel experience
- Cooking classes, trekking, elephant encounters, and cultural immersion matter more to you than nightlife and luxury shopping
- You are a digital nomad or remote worker — Chiang Mai has an excellent infrastructure for this
- You are staying longer (a week or more) and want to go deeper into one place

## The Question Most People Are Not Asking

The standard comparison treats Bangkok and Chiang Mai as interchangeable bases you rotate between. But the more useful question is: **what kind of trip do you actually want to have?**

A week in Bangkok and a week in Chiang Mai is not twice the Thailand experience. It is two half-experiences, with two days lost to travel, two accommodation check-ins, and the constant sense that you are rushing through things.

If you have 10 days and you genuinely love cities — stay in Bangkok. You will not run out of things to do. You will not wish you had gone north.

If you have 10 days and Bangkok sounds exhausting by day five — base in Chiang Mai. Do a day trip to Bangkok for any one thing that is uniquely there. Come back to the north for the rest.

## Combining Both: The Right and Wrong Ways

**The wrong way:** Bangkok 3 nights → Chiang Mai 3 nights → beach 4 nights. You will see a lot and experience little.

**The right way for most travellers with 2 weeks:** Bangkok 4–5 nights (use it intensively — temples, food, one or two day trips) → Chiang Mai 4–5 nights (base for the north — day trip to Doi Inthanon, one cooking class, one elephant sanctuary visit) → Koh Samui or Krabi 4–5 nights.

**The right way for a relaxed trip:** Pick one base and do it properly. Bangkok for the city experience. Chiang Mai for the cultural and natural experience. Go deep rather than wide.

## Getting Between Bangkok and Chiang Mai

- **Flight:** 1 hour 10 minutes, $30–80 one-way on Thai AirAsia or Nok Air. The right choice for most people.
- **Train:** 12–13 hours overnight in a sleeper car. Comfortable, surprisingly pleasant, and a legitimate travel experience if you have the time and enjoy slow travel. The overnight express leaves Bangkok Hua Lamphong at approximately 6pm.
- **Bus:** 9–11 hours. Not recommended unless the train is booked out — it is slower and less comfortable.

## If You Are Coming for Medical Care

If your primary purpose in Thailand is medical care, this decision is straightforward: **Bangkok**.

All of Thailand's JCI-accredited international hospitals are in Bangkok. Bumrungrad, Samitivej, and BangPakok 9 are all in the Sukhumvit corridor. Chiang Mai has no hospital at the same tier for international medical tourists.

What you can do is structure your trip so that your recovery period — after you are medically cleared and mobile — includes time in Chiang Mai if that is the kind of environment that appeals to you for recuperation. A week in a quiet Chiang Mai guesthouse at the end of a medical trip is genuinely restorative.

I plan this combination for clients regularly. The structure is straightforward: Bangkok for the medical work and early recovery, Chiang Mai for the final week of rest if appropriate.

## The Honest Summary

Bangkok is where you go when you want to experience Thailand at full intensity. Chiang Mai is where you go when you want to experience Thailand at human pace.

Both are worth your time. The question is which version of Thailand fits the version of you that will be on this trip.
