Bringing someone with you for major surgery is not a luxury.

For a significant proportion of procedures — cardiac surgery, major orthopaedic work, complex oncology treatment — having a family member present is clinically beneficial. It reduces patient anxiety, improves medication adherence, and means that post-operative instructions are heard and retained by at least two people rather than one.

Practically, it also means that when you are discharged and cannot yet manage your own luggage, there is someone to manage it. When you need to eat but cannot easily get to food, there is someone to get it. When you want to say "I'm scared" without saying it to a hospital coordinator, there is someone who understands.

Here is how to plan a two-person medical trip to Bangkok well.

## Accommodation: The Most Important Decision for Two People

The accommodation question is different for two people than for one. For a solo patient, you are optimising for medical proximity, elevator access, and room service. For two people, you are also optimising for the companion's livability during the days when you are in hospital — which may be five to seven days.

**Private room at the hospital:** All three major international hospitals (Bumrungrad, Samitivej, BangPakok 9) offer private rooms that accommodate one accompanying person. The companion gets a fold-out or rollaway bed, access to the room's bathroom, and can eat from the hospital restaurant. For the initial hospital stay, this is the right setup — the companion is on-site, which is particularly important in the first 24–48 hours post-surgery.

**Serviced apartment for the recovery period:** Once you are discharged, a hotel room that was adequate for one becomes cramped for two. A one-bedroom serviced apartment in the Sukhumvit area — particularly around Soi 1–11 for Bumrungrad patients — provides a proper bed for the companion, a separate space for rest, a small kitchen for preparing light meals, and a living area where neither person feels trapped.

Serviced apartments suitable for this purpose typically cost $80–150/night and are significantly more comfortable than equivalent hotel rooms at the same price point.

## What the Companion Actually Does

This is the question most companions have not thought through before they land, and the honest answer has two parts.

**During the hospital stay:**

The companion's primary role is presence. Being there when you wake up from surgery. Being there for the difficult first evening when the anaesthesia has worn off and the pain management is being calibrated. Being there to receive post-operative instructions from the surgeon and nurses when you are not in a state to absorb them yourself.

Beyond presence, the companion helps with: communicating preferences to nursing staff, keeping family at home updated, managing your personal belongings during the transition to recovery accommodation, and handling any paperwork that requires a signature.

What the companion does not need to do: the international patient coordinators at Bangkok's major hospitals handle most practical logistics. The companion does not need to be the liaison with the hospital system — that is what the coordinator is for.

**During Bangkok itself:**

The companion will have significant free time while you are in hospital and resting. Bangkok is one of the world's great cities for an unplanned day. Within a 20-minute radius of Bumrungrad and Samitivej:

- **Chatuchak Weekend Market** (Saturday and Sunday) — one of Asia's largest markets. A full morning's activity.
- **The Chao Phraya river and old Bangkok** — the river express boat, Wat Pho, the Grand Palace area, the floating flower market. A long day.
- **Ari neighbourhood** — a five-minute BTS ride north of the hospital area, with excellent coffee shops, local restaurants, and a calm pace. Good for a morning of wandering.
- **Thai massage** — a genuine cultural institution, not a tourist trap, at the right places. An hour in the afternoon.
- **Or Tor Kor Market** — the city's best fresh produce market, adjacent to Chatuchak. Extraordinary for food. An hour and a half.

Most companions come home from a medical trip having had a better time than they expected. The city is generous to people exploring it slowly and without a rigid itinerary.

## The Combined Budget for Two People

The most common question about bringing a companion is cost. Here is a realistic two-person budget for a three-week medical trip (procedure + recovery):

| Item | Estimated cost (USD) |
|------|----------------------|
| Return flights (2 people, economy, UK example) | $1,400–2,200 |
| Hospital private room (7 nights, companion bed included) | Included in hospital costs |
| Serviced apartment (14 nights, 1-bed) | $1,100–2,100 |
| Food for two people (mix of restaurant and room service) | $600–900 |
| Companion's Bangkok activities and transport | $300–500 |
| Shared transport (airport, follow-up appointments) | $150–250 |
| **Total additional cost of bringing a companion** | **~$2,500–4,000** |

The companion adds cost, but not as much as most people assume. The flights are the largest variable — if the companion can use points, works remotely and brings their laptop, or was planning a holiday to Asia anyway, the net cost reduces significantly.

What the companion gives in return — for a major surgery — is not easily valued. It is worth the calculation.

## Planning the Companion's Experience

The best two-person medical trips are ones where the companion has their own light itinerary built in advance — not because the trip is about them, but because companions who have nothing to do become anxious and restless, which is counterproductive for both people.

I plan both halves of this for every two-person client engagement: the patient's medical coordination, and a light itinerary for the companion covering the days when they are free. It costs nothing extra. It simply means that when the companion is sitting in the hospital café at 9am wondering what to do with the day, they already have a plan.
