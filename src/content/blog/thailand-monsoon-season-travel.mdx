Avoid Thailand during rainy season. That is the standard advice. It is half right.

The rain varies significantly by region. The rain itself is rarely what people imagine. And the financial case for green season is strong enough to change the calculus entirely.

## Thailand Has Multiple Climates

Thailand runs roughly the length of Western Europe from north to south. The climate in Bangkok is not the same as Chiang Mai, which is not the same as Phuket, which is not the same as Koh Samui. The travel industry collapses this into "rainy season" and "dry season" because it is easier to sell. The reality is more useful than that.

## Month by Month, Region by Region

**Bangkok and Central Thailand**

- November to February: Cool season. 25 to 30°C, low humidity, minimal rain. The most comfortable months. Peak prices.
- March to May: Hot season. 35 to 40°C, increasingly humid. Not ideal for long days of sightseeing.
- June to October: Wet season. Regular afternoon rain. Mornings often clear. Temperature drops slightly to 30 to 34°C. October and September see the most reliable rain.

**Northern Thailand (Chiang Mai)**

- November to February: Perfect. Cool evenings at 15 to 18°C, clear skies, excellent mountain visibility.
- March to May: Smoke season. Agricultural burning in the surrounding hills creates haze that is genuinely bad for air quality. This is the one period I actively advise against visiting Chiang Mai.
- June to October: Green and lush, afternoon rain, cool evenings. Less popular but genuinely beautiful. The waterfalls are full.

**Gulf Coast Islands (Koh Samui, Koh Phangan, Koh Tao)**

The Gulf has an inverted wet season. The northeast monsoon brings rain from October to January, particularly November and December. This catches visitors by surprise because those months are dry everywhere else.

- January to September: Generally good, with April to September excellent.
- October to December: Risky. Some years are fine. Some years are genuinely bad.

**Andaman Coast (Phuket, Krabi, Koh Lanta)**

The southwest monsoon hits from May to October. This side faces the Indian Ocean and the monsoon is more sustained.

- November to April: Excellent. Clear water, calm seas, ideal beach conditions.
- May to October: Wet season. Mornings often usable, but seas can be rough and some boat services are suspended.

## What the Rain Actually Looks Like

The imagined monsoon is a week of grey sky and continuous downpour. The actual monsoon in Bangkok or Chiang Mai looks like this: a clear, sunny morning. Clouds building in the afternoon. A heavy downpour between 3pm and 6pm, sometimes 30 minutes, sometimes two hours. Then it clears. Night is pleasant.

This is manageable. You do not plan outdoor activities for 3 to 5pm. You find somewhere with a good view and wait it out. Then it is gone.

The Andaman coast in peak monsoon (July to August) is more sustained. Seas are rough. If island-hopping by boat is your plan, this is not the right time. On land in Phuket or Krabi it is still viable, but plan accordingly.

## The Financial Case

Accommodation prices drop 30 to 50 percent during wet season at most properties. Flights are cheaper. Popular spots are less crowded.

A Koh Samui resort that costs 300 USD per night in February costs 150 to 180 USD in June. The beach in June has more cloud and occasional rain. For many travellers, the saving is worth the tradeoff.

Bangkok in the wet season is a particularly good deal. The city is beautiful in the rain. The rooftop bars have dramatic storm views. You are not competing with high-season crowds for tables. The rain does not stop you doing anything in Bangkok the way it can limit beach days.

## When Not to Go

- Chiang Mai in March to April: Air quality is bad enough to affect health. If you have respiratory conditions or are post-surgery, avoid.
- Koh Samui in November to December: Real risk of extended bad weather. If your trip depends on beach time, this is not the time.
- Phuket and Krabi in August to September: Peak monsoon. Seas rough, some day trips cancelled. Fine for a resort stay, not ideal for diving or island-hopping.

## Packing for Wet Season

- A compact umbrella is more useful than a rain jacket. The heat makes jackets uncomfortable.
- Waterproof sandals or shoes that dry quickly.
- A dry bag or waterproof phone case if you are doing anything on water.
- Loose, quick-dry clothing. Humidity makes cotton impractical.

## The Summary

Best time for Bangkok: November to February. September to October and June to July are perfectly viable and significantly cheaper.

Best time for Chiang Mai: November to February. Avoid March to May.

Best time for Phuket and Krabi: November to April. May to October is viable but accept the weather uncertainty.

Best time for Koh Samui: January to September. Avoid October to December.

If your dates fall in the wet window: go anyway. Plan your mornings, stay flexible in the afternoons, and enjoy paying half the high-season rate.
