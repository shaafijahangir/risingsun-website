Everyone tells you to avoid Thailand during rainy season.

They are half right, for reasons most of them cannot fully explain.

The full picture is more useful: Thailand's rainy season varies significantly by region, the rain itself is rarely what people imagine, and the financial case for travelling in green season is strong enough that it changes the calculus entirely for the right traveller.

Here is the complete breakdown.

## Thailand Has Multiple Climates, Not One

Thailand is a long country — roughly the length of Western Europe from north to south. The climate in Bangkok is not the same as the climate in Chiang Mai, which is not the same as Phuket, which is not the same as Koh Samui.

The travel industry simplifies this into "rainy season" and "dry season" because it is easier to market. The reality is more granular.

## Month-by-Month, Region by Region

**Bangkok and Central Thailand**

- **November to February:** Cool season. 25–30°C, low humidity, minimal rain. The most comfortable months for Bangkok. Peak tourist season, highest prices.
- **March to May:** Hot season. 35–40°C, increasingly humid. The hot season is genuinely uncomfortable, particularly March to May. Not ideal for long days of sightseeing.
- **June to October:** Wet season. Regular afternoon rain, occasionally heavy. Mornings are often clear. Temperature drops slightly (30–34°C) and humidity is high. October and September have the most reliable rain.

**Northern Thailand (Chiang Mai)**

- **November to February:** Perfect. Cool evenings (15–18°C), clear skies, excellent visibility in the mountains. The best time to visit.
- **March to May:** Smoke season — agricultural burning in the surrounding hills creates haze that is genuinely problematic for air quality and visibility. March to April is the worst period; this is the one time of year I actively advise against Chiang Mai.
- **June to October:** Green and lush, regular afternoon rain, cool evenings. Less popular but genuinely beautiful. The waterfalls are full. The mountains are green.

**Gulf Coast Islands (Koh Samui, Koh Phangan, Koh Tao)**

The Gulf of Thailand has an inverted wet season compared to the rest of Thailand. The northeast monsoon brings rain to the Gulf Coast from **October to January** — particularly November and December, which can see extended wet periods. This catches many travellers by surprise because these months are dry everywhere else.

- **January to September:** Generally good on the Gulf Coast, with April–September being excellent
- **October to December:** Riskier — particularly for Koh Samui and Koh Phangan. Some years are fine; some years are genuinely bad.

**Andaman Coast (Phuket, Krabi, Koh Lanta)**

The southwest monsoon hits the Andaman coast from **May to October**. This side of Thailand faces the Indian Ocean, and when the monsoon arrives it is more sustained than the Gulf side.

- **November to April:** Excellent. Clear water, calm seas, ideal beach conditions.
- **May to October:** Wet season. The rain is not constant — mornings are often clear and the beach is usable — but seas can be rough and some boat services are suspended for safety.

## What the Rain Actually Looks Like

The imagined monsoon is a week of grey sky and continuous downpour. The actual monsoon in most of Thailand is different.

Typical wet-season day in Bangkok or Chiang Mai: morning is often clear and sunny, temperature reasonable. Mid-afternoon, clouds build. Between 3pm and 6pm, a heavy downpour — sometimes 30 minutes, sometimes two hours. Evening clears. Night is pleasant.

This is genuinely manageable. You do not plan outdoor activities for 3–5pm. You find somewhere with a good view and wait it out with a coffee. The rain is dramatic and satisfying when it comes, and then it is gone.

The Andaman coast during peak monsoon (July–August) is more sustained. Seas are rough, and if island-hopping by boat is your plan, this is not the right time. For Phuket or Krabi on land, it is still viable — but plan accordingly.

## The Financial Case for Green Season

Accommodation prices in Thailand drop 30–50% during the wet season at most properties. Flights are significantly cheaper. Popular beaches and temples are notably less crowded.

A week at a good Koh Samui resort that costs $300/night in February costs $150–180/night in June. The beach experience in June is not identical to February — there is more cloud, occasional rain — but it is not dramatically worse. For many travellers, the financial saving is worth the meteorological tradeoff.

Bangkok in the wet season is a particularly good deal. The city is beautiful in the rain — the streets glisten, the rooftop bars have dramatic storm views, and you are not competing with high-season crowds for tables at popular restaurants. The rain does not stop you doing anything in Bangkok in the way it can limit beach days.

## When Not to Go

There are genuine no-fly periods if you have specific objectives:

- **Chiang Mai in March–April:** Air quality is bad enough to affect health. If you have respiratory conditions or are post-surgery, avoid.
- **Koh Samui in November–December:** Real risk of extended bad weather. If your trip depends on beach time, this is not the time.
- **Andaman coast (Phuket/Krabi) in August–September:** Peak monsoon. Seas are rough and some day trips are cancelled. Fine for a city-style resort holiday; not ideal for diving or island-hopping.

## The Practical Packing Adjustments

For wet season Thailand:

- A small compact umbrella is more useful than a rain jacket (the heat makes jackets uncomfortable; the umbrella handles the brief downpours)
- Waterproof sandals or shoes that dry quickly
- A dry bag or waterproof phone case if you are doing anything on water
- Loose, quick-dry clothing — the humidity makes cotton impractical

Nothing on this list requires special preparation. It is the same adjustment you would make for a summer trip to London.

## The Summary for Planning

The best time for Bangkok: November–February. But September–October and June–July are perfectly viable and significantly cheaper.

The best time for Chiang Mai: November–February. Avoid March–May.

The best time for Phuket/Krabi: November–April. May–October is viable but accept the weather uncertainty.

The best time for Koh Samui: January–September. Avoid October–December.

If your dates are fixed and they fall in the "wet" window — go anyway. Plan the mornings, be flexible about afternoons, and enjoy the fact that you are paying half the high-season rate.

If your dates are flexible — aim for November to February for the islands, and note that Bangkok works well in nearly any month.
