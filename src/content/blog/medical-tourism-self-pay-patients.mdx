## Why Bangkok Works for Self-Pay Patients

The cost differential between Bangkok's top hospitals and equivalent private care in the UK, US, or Australia is not marginal. It is structural.

Thai hospitals operate with lower labour costs, lower administrative overhead, and a healthcare financing model that does not involve insurance company negotiations, billing intermediaries, or the kind of charge inflation that drives US healthcare costs to their current levels.

A procedure that costs $40,000–50,000 as a self-pay patient in the US typically costs $8,000–15,000 at Bumrungrad or Samitivej — with no reduction in clinical quality. The hospital is often better than what you would access at the same self-pay price in your home country.

The savings are large enough that even after including flights, accommodation, and a full recovery stay, the total Bangkok trip cost is typically 40–60% less than the equivalent procedure at home.

## Getting a Cost Estimate Before You Commit

This is the step most people skip, and the reason most people have inflated expectations about what Bangkok will cost.

A cost estimate is not a booking. It is a document. You send your medical records, imaging, and diagnosis to the hospital's international patient department. They review it with the relevant specialist and provide a written estimate — procedure cost, anaesthesia, inpatient stay, post-operative medication, follow-up appointments. Everything itemised.

Both Bumrungrad and Samitivej provide formal written estimates for international self-pay patients. The estimate is not legally binding, but it is typically accurate within 10–15% for uncomplicated cases. For complex cases, the estimate will include a range and note which factors could push costs higher.

## The Insurance Question

Most standard travel insurance does not cover pre-planned medical procedures. It covers unexpected emergencies — if you have a heart attack in Bangkok while on holiday, your travel insurance may cover the emergency treatment. If you fly to Bangkok specifically to have elective surgery, your travel insurance policy almost certainly excludes it.

What you need for a planned medical trip is one of two things:

**Medical travel insurance:** A specialist policy that covers elective procedures abroad. These exist, they are more expensive than standard travel insurance, and they typically require you to declare your procedure and receive approval before you travel. Premiums depend on the procedure type and your health history.

**Post-operative complication coverage:** Some policies do not cover the planned procedure itself but will cover unexpected complications that arise during or after it. This is worth having even if you cannot get full medical travel insurance.

Ask your insurance broker specifically about overseas elective procedure coverage before you travel. Do not assume your existing policy covers it — confirm in writing.

## How Thai Hospitals Handle Self-Pay Patients

Bangkok's international hospitals are well set up for direct-pay international patients. The process is straightforward:

1. **Written cost estimate** before you travel (see above)
2. **Deposit on admission** — typically 30–50% of the estimated procedure cost, payable by international bank transfer or credit card
3. **Balance on discharge** — paid by the same methods
4. **Itemised receipt** — provided on discharge, suitable for potential insurance reimbursement claims if applicable

The hospitals issue detailed receipts that meet international insurance standards. If you have any possibility of partial reimbursement through a health savings account, FSA, or international health policy, the documentation you receive from a Bangkok hospital is typically sufficient to support a claim.
