Koh Samui or Phuket is the most common island decision question I get asked, and after two decades of sending people to both, I have a reliable answer.

The honest version is: it depends on exactly three things. Get these three right and the decision is obvious.

## The Three Questions

**1. Do you want a big island with lots of infrastructure, or a smaller island that feels more contained?**

Phuket is large — about 543 square kilometres, roughly the size of Singapore. It has an international airport with direct flights from Europe, Australia, and across Asia. It has major resorts, shopping centres, international hospitals, and a coastline long enough that the quiet beaches and the party beaches exist miles apart and barely notice each other.

Koh Samui is significantly smaller — about 228 square kilometres. It also has its own airport (though with limited direct international connections; most visitors connect through Bangkok). It feels like an island. You can drive around it in an hour. The distances between things are smaller, the pace is slower, and the sense that you have arrived somewhere distinct is stronger.

**2. What matters more — beach quality or overall infrastructure?**

Phuket's best beaches — Kata Noi, Kamala, Natai on the north end — are among the best in Thailand. Its worst beaches (Patong, on a busy weekend) are chaotic and overcrowded. The variance is large.

Koh Samui's beaches are consistently good to excellent. Chaweng is lively and developed. Bophut and Mae Nam on the north coast are quieter, more local-feeling, and genuinely beautiful. Bang Kao on the south is largely undiscovered. There is less variance — the floor is higher.

**3. Do you want nightlife access, or would you prefer it further away?**

Patong in Phuket is one of Southeast Asia's most intense nightlife districts. It is also entirely avoidable — there are beautiful resorts in Kamala or Rawai where you would not know it exists. But if one person in your party wants access to it and another wants to avoid it entirely, the geography of Phuket makes this manageable.

Koh Samui's nightlife centres on Chaweng. It is livelier than many people expect for a smaller island, but it is not Patong. If you want a quieter holiday, staying in Bophut or Mae Nam makes Chaweng feel like a distant rumour.

## Who Phuket Is For

**First-time visitors to Thai islands** who want the full infrastructure package — reliable restaurants at every price point, diving and snorkelling operations, day trips to Phi Phi and Phang Nga Bay, international hospitals if anything goes wrong, and a direct flight home from the island airport.

**Groups with mixed interests.** The size of Phuket accommodates people who want different things without requiring compromise. The beach contingent and the sightseeing contingent and the people who want a spa and a quiet dinner can all be in the same resort and have completely different days.

**Anyone making a side trip to Phi Phi Islands.** Phi Phi is a day trip or overnight from Phuket. It is harder to reach from Koh Samui.

**Divers.** The Similan Islands, one of the world's premier dive destinations, are accessible from Khao Lak — a 90-minute drive north of Phuket. This is a Phuket-based itinerary.

## Who Koh Samui Is For

**Couples looking for a contained, romantic holiday.** Koh Samui has an exceptional concentration of boutique resorts — particularly on the quieter north and south coasts — that offer seclusion, infinity pools, and a quality of experience that Phuket matches only at its most expensive properties.

**Families with younger children.** The calmer sea conditions on Koh Samui's north coast during much of the year, the smaller distances, and the generally less frenetic atmosphere make it easier with children.

**Repeat visitors who have done Phuket.** If you have been to Phuket twice and want something that feels different, Koh Samui is the natural step. It rewards more exploration and rewards returning with knowledge of where the good things are.

**Anyone who wants to add a trip to Koh Phangan or Koh Tao.** These smaller islands are a short ferry from Koh Samui. The three islands together form a natural itinerary.

## What Both Are Bad At

Neither island is Bangkok. For anyone coming to Thailand primarily for cultural experience — temples, markets, street food culture — the islands are add-ons, not destinations. A week in Bangkok plus four days on an island is consistently better than ten days on an island for most first-timers.

Neither island is particularly cheap compared to mainland Thailand. Resort accommodation on both is international pricing. Street food and local restaurants are still excellent value, but if your budget is very tight, the islands strain it more than Bangkok does.

## The Third Option Nobody Mentions

Krabi.

Krabi mainland (Ao Nang) and its surrounding islands — Railay Beach, Koh Lanta, Koh Phi Phi (from the Krabi side) — offer something neither Phuket nor Koh Samui does: dramatic limestone karst scenery with beach quality that matches either island.

Ao Nang is less developed than Phuket's tourist areas and less remote than Koh Samui. It is accessible by air from Bangkok (about 1h15m). It is spectacular.

If you have been to Phuket and Koh Samui and want something that will genuinely surprise you, Krabi is what I recommend.

## The Seasonal Factor

Thai island weather is seasonal and the seasons are not the same across the Gulf and Andaman coasts.

**Phuket and Krabi** (Andaman coast): Best November–April. Monsoon season May–October — rain, rough seas, some resort closures. November to February is peak season with corresponding peak pricing.

**Koh Samui and Koh Phangan** (Gulf of Thailand coast): Best February–September. The Gulf's rain pattern is inverse to the Andaman — their heavy rain comes October–January. Koh Samui is one of the few Thai islands where a December visit is genuinely risky from a weather perspective.

This matters: if you are visiting in December, Phuket is the correct answer regardless of the Samui-vs-Phuket question.
