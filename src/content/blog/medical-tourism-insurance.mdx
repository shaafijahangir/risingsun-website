Most people book the trip, then discover what their policy excludes. Do it the other way: understand your coverage before you commit, then fill the gaps.

## Standard Travel Insurance

Standard policies cover emergency treatment for unexpected illness or injury. They cover evacuation if local care is inadequate (Bangkok's hospitals rarely are). They cover trip cancellation and lost luggage.

They do not cover any procedure you planned before travelling. Standard small print reads: "We do not cover medical treatment that is the reason for, or a planned purpose of, your trip." If you are flying to Bangkok specifically for surgery, that surgery is excluded.

Travel without insurance entirely is wrong. But a standard policy is not the right policy.

## Medical Travel Insurance

These specialist policies are designed for planned procedures overseas. A good one covers:

- The declared procedure, subject to pre-approval
- Complications during the trip and after you return home
- Extended recovery stay if needed
- Cancellation if your surgeon cancels or your pre-op assessment stops the procedure

What they require: full declaration of the procedure, hospital, and surgeon before the policy is issued. Medical records. Proof the facility is accredited (all three hospitals I work with qualify). Premiums of $300–800+ depending on the procedure and your health history.

Specialist providers include AXA's international health division and dedicated medical tourism insurers. Your health insurance broker can confirm what is available in your country.

## Your Existing Health Insurance (US Patients)

PPO plans sometimes have out-of-network international benefits. HMO plans almost never cover overseas care. Check your Summary of Benefits under "Out-of-Network" and "International Coverage."

Even plans with international coverage typically limit it to emergency treatment. Planned elective surgery abroad is usually excluded.

HSAs and FSAs: eligible expenses are defined by the IRS, not geography. A medically necessary procedure qualifies whether it happens in Cleveland or Bangkok. Keep your itemised hospital receipt.

Bangkok's international hospitals issue documentation that meets US insurance standards, including ICD-10 codes, diagnosis summaries, and itemised billing. You can submit these to your US insurer for any applicable reimbursement.

## What Bangkok Hospitals Provide

Bumrungrad and Samitivej issue this as standard on discharge:

- Itemised hospital bill with procedure codes
- Discharge summary signed by the treating physician
- Formal medical report suitable for home-country insurers
- ICD-10 codes for every diagnosis and procedure

These are not special requests. Keep the full documentation package.

## The Pre-Trip Checklist

1. Read your health insurance policy under "International Coverage." Call the insurer if the language is unclear. Get the answer in writing.
2. Check your travel insurance under "Pre-existing Conditions" and "Elective Procedures."
3. Decide whether you need specialist medical travel insurance. For major surgery, yes. For a minor procedure, the cost may make self-insuring rational.
4. Arrange coverage before you book flights. Travelling without disclosing your plans to your insurer does not save money. It creates uninsured risk.
5. Keep every document from Bangkok. Hospital bills, discharge summaries, prescriptions, receipts.

The insurance situation for overseas medical care is navigable. It takes more effort than standard travel insurance, but less than dealing with a gap in coverage after you return.
