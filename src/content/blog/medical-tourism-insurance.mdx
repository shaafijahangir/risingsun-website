The insurance question kills more medical tourism plans than any other obstacle.

Not because coverage is impossible to arrange — it is not. But because most people approach it backwards: they book the trip and then discover what their policy does not cover. The right order is the opposite: understand your coverage situation before you commit to anything, then fill the gaps.

Here is exactly what each type of policy typically does and does not cover for a planned medical trip to Bangkok.

## Standard Travel Insurance

**What it covers:**
- Emergency medical treatment for unexpected illness or injury during your trip
- Emergency evacuation if local medical care is inadequate (though Bangkok's hospitals are rarely inadequate)
- Trip cancellation due to covered reasons (illness before departure, natural disasters)
- Lost luggage, flight delays, and other standard travel inconveniences

**What it almost certainly does not cover:**
- Any medical procedure you planned before travelling — this is typically classified as a "pre-existing condition" or "elective procedure" and explicitly excluded
- Complications arising from a planned procedure you did not declare
- Recovery costs for a procedure your insurer was not told about

The standard policy small print almost universally contains language like: "We do not cover any medical treatment that is the reason for, or a planned purpose of, your trip." If you are flying to Bangkok specifically to have surgery, your standard travel insurance does not cover that surgery.

This does not mean you should travel uninsured. It means you need the right policy, not just a policy.

## Medical Travel Insurance (Specialist Policies)

These are policies designed specifically for people travelling for planned medical treatment. They exist, though not all insurers offer them, and they are more involved to arrange than standard travel insurance.

**What a good medical travel insurance policy covers:**
- The planned procedure itself (subject to pre-approval and declared procedure details)
- Complications arising from the procedure, both during the trip and after you return home
- Extended recovery stay if complications require a longer-than-planned trip
- Emergency evacuation if you need care beyond what Bangkok's hospitals can provide (rare)
- Cancellation if your surgeon in Bangkok cancels or your pre-operative assessment reveals you cannot safely proceed

**What these policies require:**
- Full declaration of your procedure, hospital, and surgeon before the policy is issued
- Medical records and in some cases a pre-travel health assessment
- The procedure must be performed at an accredited facility (all three hospitals I work with qualify)
- Premiums that are significantly higher than standard travel insurance — expect $300–800+ depending on the procedure type and your health history

Specialist medical travel insurers include policies from Global Health, AXA's international health division, and dedicated medical tourism insurers. Your existing health insurance broker can advise on which policies are available in your country.

## Your Existing Health Insurance (US Patients)

For American patients with employer-sponsored health insurance or individual marketplace plans, coverage for overseas planned procedures is complex and policy-dependent. Some things worth knowing:

**PPO plans vs. HMO plans:** PPO plans sometimes have out-of-network international benefits. HMO plans almost universally do not cover overseas care. Check your Summary of Benefits and Coverage document under "Out-of-Network" and "International Coverage."

**Emergency treatment vs. elective procedures:** Even plans with international coverage typically limit it to emergency treatment. Planned elective surgery abroad is usually excluded unless your plan has specific international benefits.

**Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) and Flexible Spending Accounts (FSAs):** If you pay for a qualifying medical procedure overseas, you may be able to use HSA/FSA funds. Eligible expenses are defined by the IRS, not by geography — a procedure that is medically necessary qualifies whether it is performed in Cleveland or Bangkok. Keep your itemised hospital receipt.

**Coordination of benefits:** If your US insurer has any international coverage at all, Bangkok hospitals provide documentation that meets US insurance standards. The itemised receipt from Bumrungrad or Samitivej includes diagnosis codes, procedure codes, and the format that US insurers require for claims.

## The Documentation Bangkok Hospitals Provide

This is worth knowing before you travel, because it affects what financial options are available to you after you return.

Bangkok's internationally-accredited hospitals issue:
- **Itemised hospital bill:** Lists every service, procedure, medication, and room charge with standardised codes
- **Discharge summary:** Signed by the treating physician, detailing diagnosis, procedure performed, medications prescribed, and follow-up instructions
- **Medical report:** A formal document suitable for submission to your home country insurer or government health authority
- **ICD-10 codes:** International disease classification codes that allow any insurer, anywhere, to understand what was done

These documents are issued as standard. They are not special requests. When you are discharged from Bumrungrad, you receive a documentation package. Keep it.

## The Practical Framework

Before you book anything, work through this checklist:

1. **Read your current health insurance policy under "International Coverage."** Find out specifically what is and is not covered. Call the insurer if the policy language is unclear — get the answer in writing.

2. **Check your travel insurance policy under "Pre-existing Conditions" and "Elective Procedures."** Understand what is excluded.

3. **Decide whether you need specialist medical travel insurance.** For major surgery, yes. For dental work or a minor cosmetic procedure, the calculation is different — the procedure cost may be low enough that self-insuring the risk is rational.

4. **Arrange coverage before you book flights.** Insurer non-disclosure (booking and travelling without telling your insurer what you are doing) is the most common mistake. It does not save money — it creates uninsured risk.

5. **Keep every document from Bangkok.** Hospital bills, discharge summaries, prescriptions, receipts. You may need them weeks or months after you return.

The insurance situation for overseas medical care is navigable. It requires more effort than a standard holiday insurance purchase — but it is effort worth doing before the trip, not during or after.
