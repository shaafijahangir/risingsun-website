Most medical tourists travel alone.

Not all — many bring a family member or partner. But a significant proportion of the patients I have coordinated over two decades flew to Bangkok by themselves, had their procedure, and flew home by themselves. It is not unusual. It is not reckless. With the right preparation, it is entirely manageable.

What creates difficulty is not going alone — it is going alone without a plan. This guide is that plan.

## Before You Leave

The single most important preparation for a solo medical trip is making sure that someone at home knows where you are at all times.

This sounds obvious. In practice, many solo travellers are vague about this — "I'll send you the details when I land" — and leave family members without the information they would need if they needed to act on something.

Before you fly:

- **Leave a full trip file with someone at home.** Hospital name, hospital address, hospital international patient department contact number, your local accommodation name and address, your surgeon's name, your flight details, and your travel insurance policy number.
- **Set a check-in schedule.** A quick message at the same time each day removes a great deal of anxiety on both sides. Miss a check-in, and your contact knows something is wrong.
- **Have your travel insurance documentation on your phone.** Not buried in email — in a folder you can find in under 30 seconds.

## Arriving Alone

The arrival at Suvarnabhumi Airport is straightforward. The international patient departments at Bumrungrad, Samitivej, and BangPakok 9 all offer airport transfer services — a driver meets you in arrivals with your name, takes your bags, and gets you to the hospital or your accommodation without you navigating anything.

For solo travellers, I arrange this as standard. The pickup is pre-booked, the driver knows your name, and the first hour in Bangkok does not involve working out a SIM card and the Grab app while jet-lagged.

Once in your accommodation: unpack, eat, rest. Do not try to go anywhere. The adjustment after a long flight is real, and you are there to have medical care, not to squeeze in a temple visit.

## At the Hospital

Bangkok's internationally-accredited hospitals are well set up for solo international patients. The international patient departments at Bumrungrad and Samitivej are staffed specifically to manage foreign patients who arrive without local support.

**What the hospital provides for solo patients:**
- English-speaking international patient coordinators who escort you between departments for appointments
- Assistance completing paperwork
- Communication of post-operative instructions in writing, in English, so nothing depends on you catching everything verbally while coming out of anaesthesia
- Patient liaison contact numbers you can call from your room at any hour

**What you should arrange in advance:**
- Pre-admission paperwork completed before you land, so the arrival at hospital is straightforward
- Confirmation that your room is on a floor accessible by elevator (not an issue at modern hospitals but worth verifying)
- A method of communication with someone at home during and immediately after the procedure — many patients ask a coordinator to send a brief message to a family member once they are out of surgery

## The Post-Discharge Period

The first 24–48 hours after discharge are the most logistically challenging for solo patients. You are mobile but not fully functional, you may be managing mild pain, and you need to get from the hospital to your accommodation without incident.

For this transition, I arrange a medically-equipped transfer for every solo client — not a standard taxi, but a vehicle with a driver who understands post-discharge patients and is not going to drive aggressively.

At your accommodation, the priorities are:

**Medication management.** Your discharge pack will include medications with written instructions. A solo patient needs to understand these clearly before leaving the hospital — not in the taxi, not at the accommodation. Ask the coordinator to walk through the medications with you before discharge.

**Wound care.** For procedures that require dressing changes, confirm before discharge whether you can manage this yourself, whether your accommodation has a nurse on call, or whether you need a daily appointment at a nearby clinic. Several serviced apartments near Bumrungrad have relationships with community nurses for exactly this purpose.

**Food.** Room service is important. Before you book your accommodation, verify that it has 24-hour room service or at minimum a restaurant that covers dinner and breakfast. In the first days post-discharge you are not going to want to go out to find food.

## Managing Mobility Limitations

Different procedures affect mobility differently. A dental procedure leaves you fully mobile within hours. A knee replacement means you are walking with a frame for the first week. A cardiac procedure means limited exertion for longer.

For procedures that significantly limit mobility, solo travel requires additional planning around:

**Bathroom access.** A walk-in shower or wet room is strongly preferable to a bath with a high step. A grab rail is useful. These are not luxury requests — they are practical requirements. I include bathroom specification in every accommodation I book for solo clients post-major-surgery.

**Elevator access.** Your room must be reachable without stairs. The hotel may have elevators but if your room requires two flights of stairs to reach it from the lobby, that is not acceptable for a solo patient with limited mobility.

**Getting to follow-up appointments.** You will have at least one post-operative check before you are cleared to fly home. For a solo patient with limited mobility, this means a booked vehicle, not a street-hailed taxi.

## The Support You Actually Need

Solo medical tourists consistently tell me the same thing: the anxiety before the trip is significantly worse than the trip itself.

The practical challenges of travelling alone for surgery are real, but they are all solvable with pre-planning. What creates actual difficulty is improvising — arriving without a transfer booked, choosing accommodation without checking the bathroom configuration, not having medication instructions in writing, not having a contact at the hospital who knows your name.

With the right preparation in place, a solo medical trip to Bangkok is not notably more stressful than a solo business trip. The hospital knows who you are. Your accommodation is set up for you. Your medication is organised. Your follow-up is booked. Your flight home is timed correctly.

What you do not have is someone to hold your hand during the anxious moments. That is the genuine difference. It is manageable. Many thousands of people manage it every year.
