Most medical tourists travel alone. A significant proportion of the patients I have coordinated over two decades flew to Bangkok by themselves, had their procedure, and flew home by themselves. It is not unusual. It is not reckless. With the right preparation, it is entirely manageable.

What creates difficulty is not going alone. It is going alone without a plan.

## Before You Leave

The single most important preparation is making sure someone at home knows where you are at all times.

Before you fly, leave a full trip file with someone: hospital name and address, international patient department phone number, your accommodation name and address, your surgeon's name, your flight details, and your travel insurance policy number. Set a daily check-in time. A missed check-in means something is wrong. Keep your travel insurance documentation in a folder on your phone, not buried in email.

## Arriving Alone

The international patient departments at Bumrungrad, Samitivej, and BangPakok 9 all offer airport transfer services. A driver meets you in arrivals with your name and takes you directly to the hospital or your accommodation. I arrange this as standard for every solo client. The pickup is pre-booked, the driver knows your name, and the first hour in Bangkok requires no navigation.

Once at your accommodation: unpack, eat, rest. The adjustment after a long flight is real.

## At the Hospital

Bangkok's internationally-accredited hospitals are well set up for solo international patients.

The hospital provides: English-speaking coordinators who escort you between departments, help completing paperwork, post-operative instructions in writing in English, and patient liaison contacts you can call from your room at any hour.

Arrange in advance: pre-admission paperwork completed before you land, confirmation of elevator-accessible room, and a method of communication with someone at home after surgery. Many patients ask me to send a brief message to a family member once they are out of the procedure.

## The Post-Discharge Period

The first 24-48 hours after discharge are the most logistically challenging for solo patients. You are mobile but not fully functional, managing mild pain, and need to transfer from hospital to accommodation without incident.

I arrange a medically-equipped transfer for every solo client, not a standard taxi, but a vehicle with a driver who understands post-discharge patients.

At your accommodation, three priorities:

**Medication management.** Ask the coordinator to walk through your discharge medications with you before you leave the hospital, not in the taxi, not at the apartment.

**Wound care.** Confirm before discharge whether you can manage dressing changes yourself, whether your accommodation has a nurse on call, or whether you need daily clinic appointments. Several serviced apartments near Bumrungrad have relationships with community nurses for exactly this.

**Food.** Verify before booking that your accommodation has 24-hour room service or a restaurant covering dinner and breakfast. In the first days post-discharge you will not want to go out.

## Managing Mobility Limitations

For procedures that significantly limit mobility, solo travel requires specific planning.

Your room needs a walk-in shower or wet room, not a bath with a high step. It needs to be reachable by elevator without any stair sections. Follow-up appointments need a booked vehicle, not a street-hailed taxi. I include bathroom and access specifications in every accommodation I book for solo clients after major surgery.

## The Support You Actually Need

Solo medical tourists consistently tell me the same thing: the anxiety before the trip is significantly worse than the trip itself.

The practical challenges are real, but all of them are solvable with pre-planning. What creates actual difficulty is improvising. Arriving without a transfer. Choosing accommodation without checking the bathroom. Not having medication instructions in writing. Not having a named hospital contact.

With the right preparation in place, a solo medical trip to Bangkok is not notably more stressful than a solo business trip. The hospital knows who you are. Your accommodation is configured for you. Your medication is clear. Your follow-up is booked.

What you do not have is someone to hold your hand during anxious moments. That is the genuine difference. Many thousands of people manage it every year.
