Bangkok is not one city.

The Bangkok a backpacker sees — Khao San Road, Banglamphu, the night market strip — has almost nothing in common with the Bangkok a business traveller sees, which has almost nothing in common with the Bangkok a local uses on a daily basis.

This is simultaneously what makes the city endlessly interesting and what makes it disorienting for first-time visitors. You can spend a week in Bangkok and see only one version of it without knowing the others exist.

Here is the guide to the neighbourhoods that are actually worth your time — and what each one is for.

## Sukhumvit: The City's International Spine

Sukhumvit Road runs east from central Bangkok for roughly 30 kilometres, and the city's international life plays out along it. The western end — Soi 1 through Soi 21, served by BTS Nana and Asok stations — is the most practical base for most international visitors: good transport links, proximity to Bumrungrad and Samitivej hospitals, an extraordinary density of restaurants from every culinary tradition, and accommodation from budget guesthouses to five-star hotels within a few hundred metres of each other.

**What Sukhumvit is for:**
- Medical tourism base (the hospitals are here)
- International restaurant dining
- Nightlife (Soi 11, RCA nearby)
- Practical shopping (Terminal 21 at Asok)
- Transport hub (BTS Asok, MRT Sukhumvit both stop here)

**What Sukhumvit is not:**
- The "real" Bangkok that locals use daily
- Particularly atmospheric or beautiful
- Cheap (it is the most tourist-facing part of the city)

The area around Soi 3–5 (sometimes called Arab Street) has a significant concentration of Middle Eastern and South Asian restaurants, halal food options, and a community character distinct from the rest of Sukhumvit. For medical tourists from Bangladesh or the Gulf states, this pocket of the city offers a familiar food and cultural environment within walking distance of Bumrungrad.

## Silom and Sathorn: Business Bangkok

Silom is Bangkok's financial district by day and one of its most interesting neighbourhoods by night. Silom Road itself is all business hotels and bank towers. The streets running off it — particularly Silom Soi 4 and the area around Patpong — are a different proposition.

**Bang Rak** (the area where Silom meets the Chao Phraya river) is genuinely beautiful — the old trading district of Bangkok, with colonial-era buildings, riverside restaurants, and a pace that is slower than Sukhumvit. The Sri Mariamman Temple, the Assumption Cathedral, and the Portuguese Embassy are all within a few minutes of each other here — a cross-section of Bangkok's diverse ethnic and religious history.

**What Silom/Sathorn is for:**
- Business travellers
- Jim Thompson's House (the silk baron's estate — genuinely worth visiting)
- The Chao Phraya riverfront
- Night dining and bars (Silom has several excellent restaurant streets)
- Lumpini Park (the city's main green space, excellent early morning)

## Ari: The Neighbourhood Locals Actually Want to Live In

Ari is five BTS stops north of central Sukhumvit, and it is what Bangkok looks like when it is not performing for tourists. The streets are residential, slightly cooler due to the tree canopy, and lined with independent coffee shops, small restaurants, vintage clothing stores, and local bakeries that serve the young professional population living here.

**Why tourists miss it:** There is nothing explicitly "to do" in Ari. No major temples, no tourist markets, no landmark. The point is the neighbourhood itself.

**What Ari is actually for:**
- The best independent coffee in Bangkok (consistently excellent cafés, none of them chains)
- Local restaurants serving food at Thai prices rather than tourist prices
- A walking-pace afternoon that feels nothing like central Bangkok
- Understanding how the Bangkok middle class actually lives

If you are staying in Sukhumvit for medical purposes and have a free afternoon during recovery when you are mobile but not ready for major sightseeing, Ari is twenty minutes away by BTS and is exactly the right pace.

## Thonglor and Ekkamai: Where Bangkok is Fashionable

Thonglor (Soi 55) and Ekkamai (Soi 63) are the next stations east along the BTS from the main Sukhumvit tourist area. These are Bangkok's most fashionable residential neighbourhoods — the city's upscale young population lives here, and the streets reflect it: rooftop bars, Japanese restaurants, boutique gyms, concept stores, and the city's best craft cocktail scene.

**What Thonglor/Ekkamai is for:**
- Dinner and evening drinks in a genuinely local atmosphere
- Design and independent retail (if that interests you)
- Excellent Japanese food (the Japanese expat community in Bangkok concentrates here)
- Feeling like a local rather than a tourist

**Practical note:** These neighbourhoods are walking-unfriendly in the traditional sense — the streets are wide and there is no coherent pedestrian circuit. Grab between venues is the practical approach.

## The Old Town: Rattanakosin, Chinatown, Bang Rak

Bangkok's old town is where the temples, the river, and the city's pre-modern history concentrate. Most tourists visit the Grand Palace and Wat Pho (correctly — these are extraordinary) and see nothing else.

**Rattanakosin Island** — the original Bangkok, bounded by the river and a series of canals — has the Grand Palace, Wat Pho, Wat Arun, and the National Museum within about a kilometre of each other. A full day in Rattanakosin, done slowly, is one of Bangkok's best experiences.

**Chinatown (Yaowarat)** is a few minutes south by taxi — a dense, chaotic, extraordinarily alive street market district that comes fully alive after dark. Yaowarat Road at night is one of the city's great spectacles: neon signs, seafood restaurants, gold shops, street food vendors, and a pace that makes Sukhumvit feel calm. The roast duck and dim sum here are among the city's best.

**What the old town is for:**
- Temple visits (the absolute best are here)
- Cultural history
- Chinatown dinner
- A river boat trip to see the city from the water

**What the old town is not:**
- A comfortable base for an extended stay (limited accommodation, heavy tourist concentration, inconvenient transport)

## Khao San Road: Go Once, Do Not Stay

Khao San Road is a rite of passage and not much more. The backpacker scene that made it famous in the 1990s is still there — a few hundred metres of guesthouses, bars, pad thai stalls, and vendors selling the same Chang Beer tank tops they have been selling for thirty years.

It is worth walking through. It is not worth staying in. The accommodation is mediocre, the food is priced for tourists, and the surrounding area is poorly connected to the BTS.

The neighbourhood around it — Banglamphu — is significantly better: quieter, more local, reasonable guesthouses. If you want budget accommodation near the old town, stay in Banglamphu and visit Khao San for the experience.

## Where to Base Yourself

For most international visitors:
- **Medical tourism:** Sukhumvit (Soi 1–15 for Bumrungrad proximity, Soi 21–39 for Samitivej)
- **First-time Bangkok tourist:** Silom or lower Sukhumvit — good transport, central, manageable
- **Relaxed exploration:** Ari or lower Sathorn — local feel, easy to base for a longer stay
- **Maximum experience:** Divide your time between Sukhumvit (practical) and old town (cultural)

Avoid: Khao San Road as a base. It is optimised for one type of traveller and is inconveniently located for the rest.
