Bangkok is not one city. The Bangkok a backpacker sees shares almost nothing with the Bangkok a business traveller uses, which shares almost nothing with the Bangkok a local inhabits daily. You can spend a week here and see only one version without knowing the others exist.

Here is the guide to the neighbourhoods actually worth your time.

## Sukhumvit: The International Spine

Sukhumvit Road runs east from central Bangkok for roughly 30 kilometres. The western end (Soi 1 through Soi 21, served by BTS Nana and Asok) is the most practical base for most international visitors. Good transport, proximity to Bumrungrad and Samitivej hospitals, a dense range of restaurants from every cuisine, and accommodation from budget guesthouses to five-star hotels within a few hundred metres of each other.

**Sukhumvit is for:**
- Medical tourism (the hospitals are here)
- International dining
- Nightlife (Soi 11, RCA nearby)
- Practical shopping (Terminal 21 at Asok)
- Transit (BTS Asok and MRT Sukhumvit both stop here)

**Sukhumvit is not:**
- The Bangkok locals actually use daily
- Particularly atmospheric or beautiful
- Cheap (most tourist-facing part of the city)

The area around Soi 3 to 5, sometimes called Arab Street, has a significant concentration of Middle Eastern and South Asian restaurants and halal food. For medical tourists from Bangladesh or the Gulf states, this pocket is within walking distance of Bumrungrad and offers a familiar food environment.

## Silom and Sathorn: Business Bangkok

Silom is Bangkok's financial district by day and one of its most interesting neighbourhoods by night. Silom Road itself is business hotels and bank towers. The streets running off it are a different proposition.

Bang Rak, where Silom meets the Chao Phraya, is genuinely beautiful. This is the old trading district, with colonial-era buildings, riverside restaurants, and a slower pace than Sukhumvit. The Sri Mariamman Temple, the Assumption Cathedral, and the Portuguese Embassy are all within a few minutes of each other: a cross-section of Bangkok's diverse ethnic and religious history.

**Silom and Sathorn are for:**
- Business travellers
- Jim Thompson's House (genuinely worth the visit)
- The Chao Phraya riverfront
- Night dining and bars
- Lumpini Park (excellent for early mornings)

## Ari: What Bangkok Looks Like Without Tourists

Ari is five BTS stops north of central Sukhumvit. The streets are residential, cooler due to tree cover, and lined with independent coffee shops, small restaurants, vintage stores, and local bakeries serving the young professional population that lives here.

There is nothing explicitly "to do" in Ari. No major temples, no tourist markets, no landmark. The point is the neighbourhood itself.

**Ari is for:**
- The best independent coffee in Bangkok
- Local restaurants at Thai prices
- A walking-pace afternoon that feels nothing like central Bangkok
- Understanding how the Bangkok middle class actually lives

If you are staying in Sukhumvit for medical reasons and have a free afternoon during recovery, Ari is twenty minutes by BTS and exactly the right pace.

## Thonglor and Ekkamai: Where Bangkok is Fashionable

Thonglor (Soi 55) and Ekkamai (Soi 63) are the next stations east from the main Sukhumvit tourist area. Bangkok's upscale young population lives here, and the streets reflect it: rooftop bars, Japanese restaurants, boutique gyms, concept stores, and the city's best craft cocktail scene.

**Thonglor and Ekkamai are for:**
- Dinner and drinks in a genuinely local atmosphere
- Excellent Japanese food (the Japanese expat community concentrates here)
- Design and independent retail
- Feeling like a local rather than a tourist

These neighbourhoods are not walkable in the traditional sense. The streets are wide with no coherent pedestrian circuit. Grab between venues is the practical approach.

## The Old Town: Rattanakosin, Chinatown, Bang Rak

Bangkok's old town is where the temples, the river, and the city's pre-modern history concentrate. Most tourists visit the Grand Palace and Wat Pho, then leave. That is a mistake.

Rattanakosin Island, the original Bangkok bounded by the river and canals, has the Grand Palace, Wat Pho, Wat Arun, and the National Museum within about a kilometre of each other. A full day here, done slowly, is one of Bangkok's best experiences.

Chinatown (Yaowarat) is a few minutes south by taxi. Dense, chaotic, and fully alive after dark. Yaowarat Road at night is one of the city's great spectacles: neon signs, seafood restaurants, gold shops, and street food vendors. The roast duck and dim sum here are among the city's best.

**The old town is for:**
- Temple visits (the absolute best are here)
- Cultural history
- Chinatown dinner
- A river boat trip to see the city from the water

**The old town is not:**
- A comfortable base for an extended stay (limited accommodation, poor transport connections, heavy tourist concentration)

## Khao San Road: Go Once, Do Not Stay

Khao San Road is a rite of passage and not much more. The backpacker scene from the 1990s is still there: guesthouses, bars, pad thai stalls, and vendors selling the same Chang Beer tank tops for thirty years.

Walk through it. Do not stay there. The accommodation is mediocre, the food is tourist-priced, and the area is poorly connected to the BTS.

The surrounding neighbourhood, Banglamphu, is significantly better: quieter, more local, reasonable guesthouses. If you want budget accommodation near the old town, stay in Banglamphu and visit Khao San for the experience.

## Where to Base Yourself

- **Medical tourism:** Sukhumvit Soi 1 to 15 for Bumrungrad, Soi 21 to 39 for Samitivej
- **First-time visitor:** Lower Sukhumvit or Silom: central, good transport, manageable
- **Relaxed longer stay:** Ari or lower Sathorn: local feel, quieter pace
- **Maximum experience:** Split time between Sukhumvit (practical) and the old town (cultural)

Avoid Khao San Road as a base. It is optimised for one type of traveller and inconveniently located for everything else.
